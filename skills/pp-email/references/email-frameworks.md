# Email Framework Reference

Proven email frameworks adapted for executive recruitment outreach. Each framework has specific use cases, structures, and word counts.

---

## Framework 1: Mouse Trap

**Best for**: High-intent signals, first touch, DMs, quick follow-ups

**Structure**:
1. **Observation**: Context for why you're reaching out (one sentence)
2. **Binary question**: Value-prop driven, implies your solution without stating it

**Word count**: 20-40 words

**Why it works**: Drives impulsive reply based on curiosity. Priority check — if what you mention is a priority, you get a quick response.

**Recruitment adaptation**:
- Observation ties to a strategic objective (p1)
- Question implies capability/team design challenge without mentioning recruitment

**Example pattern**:
```
{Name}, looks like {Company} is {observation from p1}.

Would it be helpful to get a clearer picture of how {role-adjacent challenge from p2} typically plays out?
```

**Variant — Mouse Trap + Context**: Add one sentence explaining how you can help after the question.

## Framework 2: Neutral Insight

**Best for**: Follow-ups, LinkedIn DMs, nurture, re-engagement

**Structure**:
1. **Reference third-party resource**: Trusted, non-branded publication
2. **Explain why they should read it**: Ties to their context
3. **Explain why you're sharing it**: Links to their business needs
4. **Optional soft ask**: Nod back to original CTA

**Word count**: 50-70 words

**Why it works**: Breaks pattern of branded content. Builds trust on neutral ground. Positions you as a resource, not a seller.

**Recruitment adaptation**:
- Share industry report, benchmark study, or thought leadership piece
- Frame as relevant to challenges surfaced in p2/p3
- Never share your own company's content

**Example pattern**:
```
{Name}, do you follow {Publication/Author}?

Given {Company}'s focus on {objective from p1}, thought you'd find their piece on {topic} interesting.

{Brief insight from piece tied to p3 behaviour}.

Worth a look?
```

## Framework 3: Vanilla Ice Cream

**Best for**: Default first touch, balanced approach when no specific signal

**Structure**:
1. **Observation**: One sentence showing you know them (personalisation)
2. **Problem/insight**: One sentence implying pain point or value prop
3. **Credibility**: How you've helped solve this for others
4. **Solution**: What you did in one sentence
5. **CTC**: Question or interest-based call-to-conversation

**Word count**: 60-80 words

**Why it works**: Creates logic and clarity. Shows you understand their situation. Plants a seed without pitching.

**Recruitment adaptation**:
- Observation from p1 strategic objective
- Problem from p2 role constraint
- Credibility from pattern language (p3) — "usually teams in this position..."
- Solution framed as reframe (p4)
- CTC is low-friction conversation starter

**Example pattern**:
```
{Name},

{Observation from p1 — strategic signal}.

Not sure if {constraint from p2} is something you're navigating.

Usually, teams in this position {status quo behaviour from p3}. But some have shifted to {reframe from p4}.

Worth exploring if this resonates?
```

## Framework 4: BAB (Before-After-Bridge)

**Best for**: Storytelling, transformation narratives, when the contrast is compelling

**Structure**:
1. **Before**: Current problem/situation they experience
2. **After**: Ideal future where problems are resolved
3. **Bridge**: How your approach connects the two

**Word count**: 60-80 words

**Why it works**: Creates emotional journey. Makes the gap tangible. Positions you as the path forward.

**Recruitment adaptation**:
- Before: Status quo behaviour (p3) and its trade-off
- After: What changes when teams think differently (reframe from p4)
- Bridge: Framed as conversation, not service pitch

**Example pattern**:
```
{Name},

Most teams at {Company}'s stage {before — p3 status quo behaviour}. Works until {trade-off from p3}.

Some have found that {after — reframe outcome from p4}.

The bridge? Usually starts with {high-level approach — market signal, team design conversation, etc.}

Worth discussing?
```

## Framework 5: Toe Dip

**Best for**: Conversation starters, light touch, testing interest

**Structure**:
1. **Observation**: Brief context
2. **Interest-based CTC**: Simple question

**Word count**: 30-50 words

**Why it works**: Simple and brief. Seeks to start a conversation, nothing more.

**Recruitment adaptation**:
- Observation from p1
- CTC references p3 pattern as question, not statement

**Example pattern**:
```
{Company} seems to be {observation from p1}.

Leaders in similar positions often find {p3 status quo}.

Does that sound similar to what you're seeing?
```

## Framework 6: Exec-to-Exec

**Best for**: Late-stage deals, been ghosted, peer-level nudge

**Structure**:
1. **Why you're showing up**: Brief explanation
2. **Team alignment**: Reinforce you understand the problem
3. **Support offer**: Close with help, not ask

**Word count**: 40-60 words

**Why it works**: Creates connection at peer level. Shows up without pressure. Offers rather than asks.

**Recruitment adaptation**:
- Best used when you've had previous contact
- Frame as "checking in on {topic}" not following up on your pitch
- Support offer relates to p4 reframe territory

**Example pattern**:
```
{Name},

I've been thinking about the {p2 constraint} challenge we touched on.

Not sure if it's still front of mind, but happy to share what I'm seeing in the market around {p4 reframe topic}.

Useful, or has the priority shifted?
```

---

## Framework 7: Thoughtful Bump

**Best for**: Follow-up when no response

**Structure**:
1. **Context reminder**: Why you reached out originally
2. **Simple question**: Ask for feedback on your note

**Word count**: 25-40 words

**Why it works**: Thoughtful by providing context. Shows you've considered their time.

**Example pattern**:
```
{Name},

Given {p1 objective}, I thought the {p4 reframe} angle would be worth discussing.

Did you have any thoughts on my earlier note?
```

---

## Framework 8: The Clarification

**Best for**: Follow-up to rephrase/clarify original outreach

**Structure**:
1. **Restate context**: Why you reached out
2. **Clarify what you do**: Make it concrete
3. **Clarify why it's relevant**: Connect to their life
4. **CTC**: Simple question

**Word count**: 50-70 words

**Why it works**: Rephrases without repeating. Makes things concrete.

**Recruitment adaptation**:
- Use when original email may have been too abstract
- "To clarify" framing disarms

**Example pattern**:
```
{Name},

Given {p1 objective}, I thought {p4 reframe territory} might be relevant.

To clarify — I spend my time helping leaders get ahead of {p2 constraint category} before it becomes a blocker.

Teams like {similar company} have found it useful for {outcome}.

Think this could help at {Company}?
```

## Framework Selection Matrix

**CRITICAL:** Mouse Trap should only be used when there is an EXPLICIT, CLEAR high-intent signal mentioned by the user. When in doubt, use Vanilla Ice Cream.

| Signal Present | Recommended Framework |
|----------------|----------------------|
| **User explicitly mentions** job posting/hiring | Mouse Trap |
| **User explicitly mentions** funding announcement | Mouse Trap |
| **User explicitly mentions** expansion/acquisition | Mouse Trap |
| **No specific signal** (most common) | **Vanilla Ice Cream** ← DEFAULT |
| General outreach, first touch | Vanilla Ice Cream |
| Compelling transformation story | BAB |
| Testing interest, light touch | Toe Dip |
| Following up (no response) | Thoughtful Bump or Neutral Insight |
| Need to clarify original outreach | The Clarification |
| Peer-level, been ghosted | Exec-to-Exec |
| Re-engaging old conversation | Neutral Insight |

**Examples:**
- ❌ "pp-email VP of Sales at Acme" → Vanilla Ice Cream (no signal mentioned)
- ❌ "Write outbound to CTO at TechCo" → Vanilla Ice Cream (no signal mentioned)
- ✅ "pp-email about their Series B announcement" → Mouse Trap (funding explicitly mentioned)
- ✅ "Email them about the VP Engineering role they posted" → Mouse Trap (job posting mentioned)

## Writing Principles (All Frameworks)

1. **About them, not you**: 80%+ of email should reference their situation
2. **One idea only**: Single thread, single ask
3. **No recruiting language**: Never mention hiring, recruitment, agency, candidates
4. **Neutral, peer-level tone**: Not pitching, exploring
5. **No buzzwords**: Avoid "synergy", "value prop", "circle back", "quick chat"
6. **7-9 seconds of attention**: Assume they'll skim
7. **High context, broad strokes**: Reference patterns and business challenges, not technical implementations. You're a recruitment expert, not a cybersecurity engineer.
8. **No greetings/closings**: All framework examples omit "Hi [Name]" and "Best, [Name]" to create pattern-interrupt

## CTC vs CTA

- **CTA (Call-to-Action)**: Asks for specific action (book a call, schedule demo)
- **CTC (Call-to-Conversation)**: Invites dialogue (worth discussing?, does this resonate?)

PP-Email always uses CTC. Goal is reply, not meeting booking.
