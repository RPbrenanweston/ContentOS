## p2 -- Day 4: Process Ownership and Compound Efficiency

**Subject:** The recruitment knowledge your agency keeps

**Body:**

{{first_name}},

Following up on my earlier note about cost structures. There is a second dimension that tends to get less attention but has significant long-term impact.

Bersin by Deloitte (2019) research shows that organisations with process-driven recruitment functions perform 2.6 times higher across talent acquisition metrics than those operating reactively. The mechanism is straightforward: each hiring cycle generates process knowledge -- screening rubrics, pipeline data, role-specific playbooks -- that improves the next cycle.

Under the agency model, that knowledge does not transfer. The screening criteria developed during a search, the candidate pipeline built, the interview calibration data -- it stays with the agency or dissipates entirely. SHRM data indicates 48% of organisations report losing institutional knowledge with each personnel transition (SHRM, 2022). The same dynamic applies to the recruitment process itself.

The question for {{company_name}} is whether your hiring volume justifies owning that process rather than renting it repeatedly.

If that is worth a conversation, happy to walk through what the compound efficiency trajectory looks like with your numbers. 15 minutes is plenty.

{{calendar_link}}

-- Brenan

## p3 -- Day 9: Surgical vs. Systemic Diagnostic

**Subject:** A diagnostic question about {{company_name}}'s hiring mix

**Body:**

{{first_name}},

One more perspective that founders at your stage tend to find useful.

Not all roles require the same recruitment approach. A VP of Engineering search and a fourth software engineer hire are fundamentally different problems. The first needs deep network access and specialist market knowledge. The second needs a well-defined process that improves with each cycle.

Industry data suggests that companies with 20-100 employees typically find 70-85% of their roles are structurally repeatable -- roles where the candidate profile is measurable, the talent market is accessible, and the process compounds with each hire (Surgical vs. Systemic whitepaper, Section 4; Crunchbase, 2024). The remaining 15-30% genuinely benefit from specialist placement, where wrong-hire costs can reach 200% of annual salary for leadership positions (SHRM, 2022).

The question is whether {{company_name}} is paying specialist rates across the board, or has already distinguished between the two categories.

If you are curious about which of your roles fall where, I have a five-question diagnostic that takes about 10 minutes. Some founders find the split surprising.

Worth a look?

-- Brenan
