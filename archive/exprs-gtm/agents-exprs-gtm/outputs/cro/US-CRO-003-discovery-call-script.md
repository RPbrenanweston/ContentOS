# US-CRO-003: Discovery Call Script

**Story:** US-CRO-003 | Write Discovery Call Script
**Agent:** CRO
**Date:** 2026-02-13
**Status:** Complete
**Call Duration:** 15-20 minutes
**Voice Compliance:** Pragmatic Operational -- per CMO Brand Voice Guide v1.0

---

## Script Philosophy

This is a consultant conversation, not a vendor pitch. The caller's role is to diagnose the prospect's recruitment structure, share relevant industry data, and determine whether infrastructure is a fit. If it is not a fit, say so. Consultative honesty builds more pipeline than aggressive closing.

**Key principle:** 80% of the call should be the prospect talking. The caller asks questions, listens for pain signals, and offers data-backed observations. Exprs is mentioned only when the prospect's situation warrants it.

---

## Pre-Call Preparation (2 Minutes)

Before the call, review:

1. **Company size** (LinkedIn, Companies House, Crunchbase): employee count, growth trajectory
2. **Recent hires** (LinkedIn job postings, careers page): volume, role types, open positions
3. **Industry vertical**: which scenario walkthroughs from Pillar B apply
4. **Previous email engagement**: which emails opened, which pillar resonated

**Prepare one custom observation** for the opening. Examples:
- "I noticed {{company_name}} has three engineering roles posted -- that suggests a growth phase."
- "Your team has grown from 30 to 50 in the past year based on LinkedIn data."
- "I saw you brought on a Head of Product recently -- curious whether that was agency-placed or internal."

| Phase | Duration | Purpose |

## Phase 1: Opening (2 Minutes)

### What to Say

> "{{first_name}}, thanks for making the time. I will keep this to 15 minutes as promised.
>
> Quick context on why I reached out. I spend most of my time working with founders and operational leaders at companies in the 20-80 employee range -- specifically on how they structure their recruitment function. Not selling recruitment services. More interested in the economics of how companies at your stage resource their hiring.
>
> Before I share anything, I am curious about how {{company_name}} currently handles it. Mind if I ask a few questions?"

### What to Listen For

- **Willingness to engage:** If the prospect is guarded, they may not be ready. Pivot to: "Happy to keep this brief -- is there one question about your recruitment costs I could answer in 2 minutes?"
- **Immediate pain signal:** If they launch into frustrations unprompted, let them talk. Take notes. This is your discovery data.

### What NOT to Say

- Do not mention Exprs by name in the opening
- Do not describe what Exprs does yet
- Do not use "I'd love to show you our platform" or equivalent
- Do not use "quick chat" or "pick your brain"

## Phase 2: Discovery Questions (8 Minutes)

### Core Questions (Ask All)

**Q1: Current Model**
> "How does {{company_name}} currently resource its hiring? Agencies, internal recruiter, founders doing it directly, some combination?"

**What to listen for:** Agency dependency level, whether they have any internal capability, who makes hiring decisions.

---

**Q2: Volume and Frequency**
> "Roughly how many placements did you make in the past 12 months? And how many do you anticipate in the next 12?"

**What to listen for:** Hiring velocity. Under 4/year = marginal infrastructure case. 5-10/year = breakeven zone. 10+/year = strong infrastructure case. (Pragmatic Costing whitepaper, Section 3.)

---

**Q3: Cost Awareness**
> "Do you have a sense of what you spent on recruitment last year -- agency fees, job boards, time costs, all in?"

**What to listen for:** Many founders do not know the total number. If they cite agency fees only, note that the full cost is higher (Layer 2-4 costs from Pragmatic Costing). If they cite a specific number, compare against CIPD/SHRM benchmarks.

**Data anchor:** "CIPD (2024) reports the average UK cost per hire at GBP 6,125, rising to GBP 19,000 for manager-level. At 20% agency fees on median salaries (Payscale, 2025), companies at your stage typically spend GBP 40,000-65,000 annually. Does that align with your experience?"

---

**Q4: Role Mix (Surgical vs. Systemic Diagnostic)**
> "Of the roles you hire for, how many are repeatable -- the same type of role you fill multiple times -- versus truly unique or specialist positions?"

**What to listen for:** This is the Pillar B diagnostic. Most ICP companies find 70-85% of roles are systemic (Surgical vs. Systemic whitepaper, Section 4). If the prospect says "all our roles are unique," probe further: "When you say unique, do you mean the candidate profile differs each time, or that it is a different function?" Often "unique" means "we have not standardised the process," not that the role is genuinely specialist.

---

**Q5: Process Retention**
> "After your last agency placement, what did you retain from the process? The screening criteria, the pipeline data, the playbooks?"

**What to listen for:** Almost always the answer is "nothing" or "just the hired person." This surfaces the knowledge retention problem (Steady-State whitepaper, Section 1.2; SHRM, 2022) without the caller stating it. Let the prospect arrive at the insight.

---

### Conditional Questions (Ask If Relevant)

**If prospect mentions internal recruiter:**
> "How does your internal recruiter spend their time? Is it mostly sourcing from scratch each time, or do they have established processes and tools that improve with each hire?"

**Purpose:** Surfaces the infrastructure gap. An internal recruiter without infrastructure is a person without a system. (Pillar A framing: infrastructure amplifies what recruiters do.)

---

**If prospect mentions satisfaction with agency:**
> "That is good to hear. One question I find useful: when you hire the same role type again -- say, another engineer -- does the second search go faster or cost less than the first?"

**Purpose:** If the answer is no, the compound efficiency argument (Bersin by Deloitte, 2019 -- 2.6x performance differential) is immediately relevant. If the answer is yes, the prospect may already be building internal capability.

---

**If prospect mentions cost pressure or budget concerns:**
> "Is it the absolute cost that concerns you, or the unpredictability? Those tend to be different problems with different solutions."

**Purpose:** Separates sticker shock (may be a pricing conversation) from structural frustration (infrastructure conversation). Routes to Pillar C appropriately.

## Phase 3: Framework Sharing (4 Minutes)

### Selecting the Right Pillar

Based on discovery responses, share **one** pillar insight. Do not present all three. Choose the one most relevant to the prospect's stated pain.

---

### If Cost Structure Is the Primary Pain (Use Pillar C)

> "Based on what you have described, it sounds like most of your recruitment spend is variable -- percentage-based fees that scale with volume and salary movement. The industry data on this is clear: at 5 placements per year with agencies at 20% of a GBP 55K median (Payscale, 2025), the three-year cost reaches roughly GBP 173,000 (Pragmatic Costing whitepaper, Section 3). With salary inflation compounding that annually (ONS, 2025), the trajectory only goes up.
>
> The alternative model is a fixed operational expense -- a recruitment function that costs the same regardless of how many placements you make or how salaries move. At GBP 36,000 per year fixed, the three-year comparison is GBP 108,000 versus GBP 173,000. But the more significant difference is that the fixed model builds process ownership -- your cost per hire actually declines with each placement rather than staying flat."

**Data reference:** Pragmatic Costing whitepaper (US-CMO-007), Section 3, Scenario 2; Payscale (2025); ONS (2025).

---

### If Process Ownership / Knowledge Loss Is the Primary Pain (Use Pillar A)

> "Based on what you have described, it sounds like each hire starts from a relatively similar baseline -- the process does not compound. Bersin by Deloitte (2019) research shows that organisations with process-driven recruitment functions perform 2.6 times higher across talent acquisition metrics than reactive ones. The difference is whether the process knowledge -- the screening rubrics, the pipeline data, the role-specific playbooks -- stays with you or leaves with the agency.
>
> The concept we work with is recruitment as operational infrastructure. Like IT systems or financial processes, it is always on, it improves over time, and you own it. The tenth hire through owned infrastructure costs less and takes less time than the first. Through agencies, the tenth hire costs the same as the first."

**Data reference:** Steady-State whitepaper (US-CMO-004), Sections 2.3 and 4; Bersin, J. (2019) *Talent Acquisition Maturity Model*. Available at: https://www.bersin.com/practice/detail.aspx?id=19844.

---

### If Role Mix / Agency Overuse Is the Primary Pain (Use Pillar B)

> "Based on what you have described, it sounds like you might be using a specialist approach for roles that are structurally repeatable. That is a common pattern -- most companies at your stage find that 70-85% of their hiring demand is systemic: well-defined roles, accessible talent markets, repeatable processes (Surgical vs. Systemic whitepaper, Section 4).
>
> The diagnostic question is which roles genuinely need specialist placement -- your VP hire, your CISO, your Head of Data -- and which roles benefit from infrastructure that compounds with each cycle. The financial difference is meaningful: paying 20% of salary for every role (SHRM, 2024) versus a fixed cost for the systemic ones and reserving agency budget for the genuinely surgical searches."

**Data reference:** Surgical vs. Systemic whitepaper (US-CMO-005), Section 4 (scenario walkthroughs); SHRM (2024) *Talent Acquisition Benchmarking Report*. Available at: https://www.shrm.org/topics-tools/news/talent-acquisition.

## Phase 4: Objection Handling (3 Minutes)

### Common Objections and Approved Responses

Refer to the full **US-CRO-004 Objection Playbook** for complete responses. Quick-reference versions below.

---

**"We already have an internal recruiter."**

> "That makes sense. An internal recruiter is a person. What we are describing is the infrastructure they operate within -- the templates, rubrics, pipeline data, and process documentation that makes them more effective with each hire. It amplifies what they already do."

*(Steady-State whitepaper, Sections 2.2-2.3)*

---

**"Agencies find great people for us."**

> "Many do. The question is not the quality of individual placements. It is what happens to the process knowledge after each one. Does the next hire benefit from the last, or does the search start from the same baseline?"

*(Steady-State whitepaper, Section 1.2; Bersin by Deloitte, 2019; SHRM, 2022)*

---

**"We only hire 2-3 people a year."**

> "At that volume, the direct cost comparison is approximately even (Pragmatic Costing whitepaper, Section 3, Scenario 1). The relevant question is growth trajectory. If you anticipate staying at 2-3 permanently, agencies are a reasonable model. If growth to 5 or more is on the horizon, infrastructure starts compounding value now."

*(Pragmatic Costing whitepaper, Section 3, Scenario 1)*

---

**"Can we try it for one month?"**

> "Infrastructure needs 90 days to compound. Month one is setup and calibration. Month two is refinement. Month three is where compound efficiency becomes visible. We recommend a 90-day commitment with clear milestones at each stage."

*(Pragmatic Costing whitepaper, Section 6)*

---

**"GBP 36K per year is expensive."**

> "It needs context. At 5 placements with agencies at 20% of GBP 55K median (Payscale, 2025), the agency cost is GBP 55K annually. Over three years with salary escalation (ONS, 2025), that reaches GBP 173K. Infrastructure over the same period: GBP 108K. The three-year difference is GBP 65K. The question for finance is not whether GBP 36K is expensive in isolation, but what the three-year trajectory looks like under each model."

*(Pragmatic Costing whitepaper, Sections 3 and 4; Payscale, 2025; ONS, 2025)*

---

**"Agencies have deeper networks for hard-to-fill roles."**

> "For genuinely specialist roles, that is correct. A CISO search, a VP hire -- those benefit from deep network access. Our framework distinguishes between those surgical placements and the 70-85% of roles that are systemic (Surgical vs. Systemic whitepaper, Section 4). The argument is not to replace agencies entirely. It is to stop paying specialist rates for repeatable work."

*(Surgical vs. Systemic whitepaper, Sections 1.1 and 4; SHRM, 2022)*

---

**"We tried RPO before and it did not work."**

> "RPO is still outsourcing -- the process is managed externally. Infrastructure is owned. Every process, dataset, and playbook belongs to your organisation from day one. If a previous RPO engagement failed to build internal capability, that is precisely the problem the infrastructure model addresses."

*(Steady-State whitepaper, Sections 2 and 3)*

---

## Phase 5: Close (2 Minutes)

### If the Prospect Is Interested

> "Based on what we have discussed, it sounds like there might be a meaningful difference between your current cost structure and what an infrastructure model would look like for {{company_name}}. The next step would be a 30-minute session where I walk through the TCO comparison using your actual numbers -- your hiring volume, your salary ranges, your current agency costs. That way you have a data-based comparison rather than a hypothetical one.
>
> Would that be useful? I can send a calendar link, or we can pick a time now."

---

### If the Timing Is Not Right

> "Understood. No pressure on timing. If it would be useful, I can send over the cost comparison framework so you have it when the question comes up again. Most founders revisit this when the next agency invoice arrives.
>
> Happy to pick this up whenever suits."

---

### If It Is Not a Fit

> "Based on what you have described, it sounds like your current model is working and your volume does not necessarily justify the infrastructure investment right now. That is an honest assessment -- not every company needs this at every stage.
>
> If growth picks up and the economics shift, worth having the conversation again. I will leave you with the industry benchmarks we discussed in case they are useful for your planning."

## Post-Call Documentation

After every discovery call, document the following in the **Sales Onboarding Handoff Template** (US-CRO-006):

1. **Primary pain signal identified** (cost, process, mix, other)
2. **Pillar that resonated most** (A, B, or C)
3. **Hiring volume** (past 12 months + anticipated)
4. **Current model** (agency-dependent, partial internal, hybrid)
5. **Objections raised** (with response used)
6. **Next step agreed** (TCO session, sent materials, not a fit)
7. **Estimated deal timeline** (if applicable)
