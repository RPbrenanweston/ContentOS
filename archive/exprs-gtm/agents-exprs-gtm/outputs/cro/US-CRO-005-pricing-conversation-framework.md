## Framework Philosophy

Never lead with price. Lead with the cost comparison frame. The goal of a pricing conversation is not to justify GBP 36K/year. It is to help the prospect understand the three-year cost trajectory under two different recruitment models, and to let the data determine which model fits their situation.

**Three rules for pricing conversations:**

1. **Show the annual maths first.** The prospect needs the comparison frame before they hear the number.
2. **Emphasise "fixed" not "cheap."** The value is predictability and compound efficiency, not a discount.
3. **Use CFO language.** Fixed vs. variable cost, operational expense, budget forecasting, cost trajectory.

---

## When to Use This Framework

- Discovery call has surfaced cost-related pain (agency fees, budget unpredictability, scaling costs)
- Prospect has asked "how much does it cost?" directly
- Prospect is comparing Exprs to agency model and needs data
- CFO or Finance Director is the decision-maker or influencer
- Post-discovery: the prospect is considering a follow-up and pricing is the gate

---

## Step 1: Frame the Comparison (Before Stating Price)

### Opening the Pricing Discussion

> "Before we discuss pricing, it might be useful to frame the comparison. Your current model is variable -- percentage-based fees that scale with both volume and salary movement. What we are proposing is a fixed operational expense: a monthly cost that stays the same regardless of how many placements you make or how salary markets move.
>
> The question is not which is cheaper in any given month. The question is what the three-year cost trajectory looks like under each model.
>
> If you are comfortable, I can walk through the numbers using your hiring volume and typical salary ranges."

### Why This Works

- Sets up TCO framing (Galorath, 2020) before price is introduced
- Uses CFO vocabulary ("variable," "fixed operational expense," "cost trajectory")
- Asks permission to proceed (consultative, not presumptive)
- Does not name a price yet

---

## Step 2: TCO Calculator Walkthrough

### Data Collection (Ask the Prospect)

Collect three numbers from the prospect:

1. **Annual hiring volume:** "How many placements did you make in the past 12 months, and how many do you anticipate in the next 12?"
2. **Blended median salary:** "What is the typical salary range for the roles you hire? We can use a blended median."
3. **Agency fee rate:** "What percentage do your agencies typically charge? Most UK agencies are in the 15-25% range (SHRM, 2024)."

### Running the Comparison

Use the prospect's numbers to populate this framework. If they cannot provide exact numbers, use the industry benchmarks as defaults.

**Default assumptions (UK market):**
- Blended median salary: GBP 55,000 (Payscale, 2025)
- Agency fee rate: 20% (SHRM, 2024)
- Annual salary escalation: 5% (ONS, 2025)
- Infrastructure cost: GBP 36,000/year (GBP 3,000/month)

## Step 3: Present the Price in Context

### Stating the Price

Only after the comparison frame is established:

> "Exprs is GBP 3,000 per month. GBP 36,000 per year. Fixed -- regardless of how many placements you make, regardless of salary movement.
>
> At your volume of [X] hires per year, that means your three-year cost is GBP 108,000 versus approximately GBP [agency total] through agencies. The difference is GBP [saving] -- plus you own the process, the data, and the playbooks.
>
> The cost per hire starts at approximately GBP 7,200 for the first placement and declines with each subsequent hire as the process compounds. By placement ten, industry research suggests it reaches approximately GBP 3,600 (Pragmatic Costing whitepaper, Section 5; Bersin by Deloitte, 2019)."

### What NOT to Say When Presenting Price

- "It is only GBP 3K a month" -- never minimise the investment
- "You will save X percent" -- never claim savings without specifying conditions
- "This pays for itself in month one" -- infrastructure ROI takes 90 days
- "Our competitors charge more" -- never reference competitors
- "Special pricing" or "limited offer" -- no pressure language

## Step 4: Cost Per Hire Decline Curve

### Explaining Compound Efficiency

> "The most important economic difference between the two models is the cost per hire trajectory.
>
> Under the agency model, cost per hire is flat. Placement number ten costs the same as placement number one. There is no learning, no process improvement, no efficiency gain.
>
> Under the infrastructure model, each placement improves the next. Bersin by Deloitte (2019) research shows organisations with process-driven recruitment achieve 15-30% reduction in cost per hire as they reach maturity. The trajectory looks approximately like this:"

| Placement # | Infrastructure CPH | Agency CPH | Gap |
|-------------|-------------------|------------|-----|
| 1 | GBP 7,200 | GBP 11,000 | Agency cheaper |
| 2 | GBP 6,500 | GBP 11,000 | Roughly even annualised |
| 3 | GBP 5,800 | GBP 11,000 | Infrastructure ahead |
| 5 | GBP 4,500 | GBP 11,000 | 59% lower |
| 10 | GBP 3,600 | GBP 11,000 | 67% lower |
| 15 | GBP 3,200 | GBP 11,000 | 71% lower |
| 20+ | GBP 3,000 | GBP 11,000 | 73% lower |

*Source: Pragmatic Costing whitepaper (US-CMO-007), Section 5. Bersin, J. (2019) *Talent Acquisition Maturity Model*. Available at: https://www.bersin.com/practice/detail.aspx?id=19844 -- 15-30% CPH reduction at process maturity.*

> "The crossover point -- where infrastructure becomes cheaper per hire than agencies -- is typically around placement three to four. After that, the gap widens with every hire."

---

## Step 5: ROI Timeline Explanation

### Setting Expectations

> "I want to be honest about the timeline. Infrastructure is not a day-one cost reduction. It is a 90-day investment with a defined return trajectory.
>
> **Month 1-3:** Setup and calibration. Processes are being established. This is the highest cost per hire period. You are investing in the foundation.
>
> **Month 4-6:** Refinement. The first placements generate data that improves the process. Cost per hire begins to decline. Screening criteria calibrate.
>
> **Month 7-12:** Compound efficiency. The infrastructure model begins to outperform the agency model on cost per hire. Role-specific playbooks are established. Pipeline depth enables faster time-to-fill. McKinsey (2024) data indicates structured hiring approaches reduce timelines by 15%.
>
> **Year 2 onward:** Mature operations. The cost per hire continues to decline. The function operates independently.
>
> This is the same investment curve as a cloud migration, an ERP implementation, or building an internal finance function. The economics favour it at your volume -- but only if the 90-day investment period is understood upfront."

## Step 6: Pricing Conversation Scenarios

### Scenario A: The Founder Who Asks "What does it cost?" Early

**Situation:** Five minutes into the conversation, the prospect asks about pricing directly.

**Response:**
> "Happy to cover that. Before I give you the number, it is more useful if I frame the comparison. Can I ask -- how many hires are you planning in the next 12 months, and what is your typical agency fee rate? That way I can show you the maths with your numbers rather than hypotheticals."

**Then proceed to Step 2 (TCO Calculator).**

---

### Scenario B: The CFO Who Wants the Bottom Line

**Situation:** The CFO wants numbers, not narrative. They are evaluating this as a budget line item.

**Response:**
> "GBP 3,000 per month. GBP 36,000 per year. Fixed. No volume escalators, no percentage-based fees, no salary-linked increases.
>
> At [their volume], your current agency cost trajectory over three years is approximately GBP [total]. Infrastructure is GBP 108,000. The difference is GBP [saving].
>
> Beyond direct cost: you own the process, the data, and the playbooks. Your cost per hire declines with each placement rather than staying flat (Bersin by Deloitte, 2019). And your recruitment budget becomes a predictable line item rather than a variable dependent on salary markets and agency availability.
>
> I can send you the three-year model in writing if that is more useful for your planning."

---

### Scenario C: The Startup Founder at Series A (4-6 Hires Planned)

**Situation:** Early-stage, cost-conscious, may not see the volume case yet.

**Response:**
> "At 4-6 hires per year, the direct cost comparison is close -- agencies and infrastructure are roughly in the same range (Pragmatic Costing whitepaper, Section 3). The decision at your stage is less about immediate savings and more about what you are building.
>
> If {{company_name}} plans to grow beyond 6 hires in the next 18 months, infrastructure starts compounding efficiency now (Bersin by Deloitte, 2019). The cost per hire declines with each placement. The processes you build today serve you at 10, 20, 50 hires.
>
> If you expect to stay at 4-6 permanently, the agency model is defensible. The question is your growth trajectory, not today's volume."

---

### Scenario D: The Prospect Comparing to an Internal Hire

**Situation:** "For GBP 36K I could just hire a junior recruiter."

**Response:**
> "You could. A junior recruiter at GBP 30-35K salary plus employer NI at 15% above GBP 5,000 (HM Government, 2024), pension, and benefits costs approximately GBP 40-45K fully loaded. That gives you a person.
>
> What it does not give you is the system: documented processes, calibrated screening rubrics, accumulated pipeline data, role-specific playbooks, interview calibration frameworks. Your junior recruiter needs those tools to be effective.
>
> Infrastructure is the system that makes your recruiter -- whether junior, senior, or the founders themselves -- more effective with each hire. The two are complementary, not substitutes."

---

### Scenario E: The Prospect Negotiating on Price

**Situation:** "Can you do GBP 2,000 a month?"

**Response:**
> "The pricing reflects the full infrastructure deployment -- processes, technology, ongoing optimisation, and support. At GBP 3,000 per month, the three-year cost is GBP 108,000. At your hiring volume of [X] per year, agencies cost approximately GBP [total] over the same period.
>
> The pricing is not negotiable on a per-month basis, but there are commitment structures we can discuss. The 90-day commitment with defined milestones is designed to give you data before a longer commitment.
>
> The question is not whether GBP 3,000 is the right number in isolation. It is whether GBP 108,000 over three years with compound efficiency is a better trajectory than GBP [agency total] with no efficiency gain."

## CFO-Focused Talking Points

Use these when the financial decision-maker is in the conversation:

1. **Fixed vs. variable budgeting:** "Your recruitment spend becomes a forecastable line item rather than a figure that fluctuates with salary markets and hiring timing."

2. **Salary escalation insulation:** "Percentage-based fees compound with wage inflation automatically (ONS, 2025). A 5% salary increase means a 5% higher agency fee with no efficiency offset. Infrastructure costs are fixed."

3. **Three-year trajectory:** "The year-one comparison may be close. The three-year comparison is not. Salary escalation and volume growth compound the difference."

4. **Budget variance reduction:** "No surprise invoices when a critical role commands a higher salary than budgeted."

5. **Capital allocation clarity:** "The recruitment function becomes a known operational expense, similar to your cloud infrastructure or accounting software."

6. **Employer NI context:** "UK employers face additional cost pressure from the April 2025 NI changes -- the employer rate increasing to 15% on salaries above GBP 5,000 (HM Government, 2024). This increases the base salary that agency percentage fees are calculated against. Fixed costs are insulated."

7. **Process ownership as asset:** "At the end of a three-year agency relationship, you own nothing. At the end of three years with infrastructure, you own documented processes, calibrated rubrics, pipeline data, and a functioning recruitment operation."

8. **Compound efficiency as margin improvement:** "Your cost per hire declines with each placement. By placement ten, research suggests 15-30% reduction versus baseline (Bersin by Deloitte, 2019). That margin improvement is permanent."

9. **Dependency elimination:** "Without owned processes, switching agencies resets institutional knowledge to zero (SHRM, 2022). Infrastructure eliminates this dependency permanently."

10. **Build vs. rent analogy:** "This is the same analysis that justified moving from outsourced bookkeeping to an internal finance function, or from on-premises servers to cloud infrastructure. The economics reach a tipping point where owned capacity outperforms rented capacity."
