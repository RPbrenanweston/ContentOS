# Pragmatic Costing: A Total Cost of Ownership Analysis for Recruitment Operations

**Why the agency invoice is not your recruitment cost -- and what to measure instead**

*Published by Exprs.io | February 2026*

---

## Executive Summary for CFOs

Your recruitment line item understates your actual recruitment cost by a significant margin. Agency placement fees -- typically 15 to 25 percent of first-year salary (SHRM, 2024) -- are the visible layer. Underneath sit three additional cost categories: salary escalation exposure (percentage-based fees compound with wage inflation), knowledge retention loss (process intelligence resets to zero after each engagement), and structural dependency (no owned capability, no competitive leverage, no alternative).

This paper applies Total Cost of Ownership methodology (Galorath, 2020) -- the same framework used to evaluate IT infrastructure, cloud migration, and outsourcing decisions -- to the recruitment function. Using industry data from SHRM (2024), CIPD (2024), Bureau of Labor Statistics (2024), and ONS (2025), it projects three-year cost trajectories for variable (agency) and fixed (infrastructure) models across five company stages. The analysis is honest: at fewer than four placements per year, agencies are cost-competitive on direct spend. At five or more, infrastructure delivers lower total cost, compound efficiency gains, and budget predictability. The numbers determine the right model for your organisation.

---

## 1. The Incomplete Metric: Cost Per Hire

The standard recruitment cost metric is cost per hire. SHRM reports the average at $4,700 (SHRM, 2024). CIPD places the UK average at GBP 6,125, rising to GBP 19,000 for manager-level positions (CIPD, 2024). These figures are useful but incomplete.

Cost per hire, as typically calculated, captures direct placement costs: agency fees, job board advertising, recruiter time. It does not capture the full economic cost of the recruitment model chosen. Organisations using Total Cost of Ownership analysis for IT procurement, real estate decisions, or manufacturing sourcing understand this distinction intuitively. A server's purchase price is not its cost. The cost includes implementation, maintenance, training, downtime, and eventual migration.

Recruitment is no different.

## 2. Four Layers of Recruitment Cost

### Layer 1: Direct Placement Cost

The visible cost. Agency fees at 15 to 25 percent of first-year salary (SHRM, 2024).

**UK example (5 placements at GBP 55K blended median):**
- Low estimate (15%): GBP 41,250 per year
- Mid estimate (20%): GBP 55,000 per year
- High estimate (25%): GBP 68,750 per year

**US example (5 placements at $120K blended median):**
- Low estimate (15%): $90,000 per year
- Mid estimate (20%): $120,000 per year
- High estimate (25%): $150,000 per year

### Layer 2: Salary Escalation Exposure

Agency fees are percentage-based. They compound with salary inflation automatically.

UK average weekly earnings growth stood at 4.5 percent in late 2025 (ONS, 2025). Technology roles saw varying growth: software engineers at 1.6 percent, though the prior year saw 8.5 percent growth (Ravio, 2025). Specialist skills in cybersecurity and machine learning carry premiums of 15 to 25 percent above general software roles.

A five percent annual salary escalation on a GBP 55,000 median role produces the following agency fee trajectory at 20 percent:

| Year | Median Salary | Agency Fee (20%) | Cumulative Increase |
|------|--------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | GBP 55,000 | GBP 11,000 | Baseline |
| 2 | GBP 57,750 | GBP 11,550 | +5.0% |
| 3 | GBP 60,638 | GBP 12,128 | +10.3% |
| 5 | GBP 66,884 | GBP 13,377 | +21.6% |

Over five years, the per-placement agency fee increases by 21.6 percent with no corresponding improvement in service quality or process efficiency. Fixed infrastructure costs are insulated from this escalation entirely.

### Layer 3: Knowledge Retention Cost

Each agency placement is a standalone transaction. The screening criteria developed, the candidate pipeline generated, the role-specific playbooks refined, and the interview calibration data accumulated -- none of this intellectual property transfers to the client. The agency retains it (for use across all clients) or it simply dissipates.

Research indicates that 48 percent of organisations lose institutional knowledge with each personnel transition (SHRM, 2022). Applied to the recruitment function: the process knowledge that could improve subsequent hires is not retained. The tenth placement through an agency costs the same as the first because the organisation starts from the same baseline every time.

This is not a failure of agency quality. It is a structural property of the transactional model. Agencies are optimised for placement volume, not client capability building. Their economic incentive is to deliver the next placement, not to make the client self-sufficient.

### Layer 4: Dependency and Switching Cost

Without owned recruitment processes, the organisation has no alternative to its current provider. Switching agencies resets knowledge to zero -- again. There is no competitive leverage because there is no internal capability to benchmark against.

The switching cost is not primarily financial. It is operational. Building internal infrastructure eliminates this dependency permanently.

## 4. Salary Escalation Protection

The agency model's percentage-based pricing creates an embedded exposure to salary inflation that compounds over time. This is not hypothetical -- UK wage data confirms the trajectory (ONS, 2025).

Over a five-year period with 5 percent annual salary growth, a company making 8 placements per year sees the following total agency cost trajectory:

| Year | Per-Placement Fee | Annual Cost (8 hires) | Cumulative Total |
|------|-------------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| 1 | GBP 11,000 | GBP 88,000 | GBP 88,000 |
| 2 | GBP 11,550 | GBP 92,400 | GBP 180,400 |
| 3 | GBP 12,128 | GBP 97,020 | GBP 277,420 |
| 4 | GBP 12,734 | GBP 101,871 | GBP 379,291 |
| 5 | GBP 13,370 | GBP 106,964 | GBP 486,255 |

The infrastructure model: GBP 36,000 per year, every year, regardless of salary movement. Five-year total: GBP 180,000.

**Five-year difference at 8 hires/year: GBP 306,255.**

## 5. The Cost Per Hire Decline Curve

The compound efficiency argument is the most important economic principle in this analysis. Each placement through owned infrastructure improves the next.

**Estimated cost per hire trajectory (infrastructure model):**

| Placement # | Estimated CPH | Efficiency Driver |
|-------------|--------------|-------------------|
| 1 | GBP 7,200 | Setup, initial calibration |
| 2 | GBP 6,500 | Process refined from first hire |
| 3 | GBP 5,800 | Screening rubric calibrated |
| 5 | GBP 4,500 | Pipeline data leveraged |
| 10 | GBP 3,600 | Mature process, multiple role types |
| 15 | GBP 3,200 | Optimised across functions |
| 20+ | GBP 3,000 | Plateau (marginal gains diminish) |

**Agency cost per hire:** GBP 11,000 at every placement. Flat. No decline. No learning.

This trajectory is supported by Bersin by Deloitte research showing 15 to 30 percent cost-per-hire reduction in organisations that achieve process maturity (Bersin by Deloitte, 2019). McKinsey data indicates that structured hiring approaches reduce timelines by 15 percent (McKinsey, 2024). Both efficiency gains are only available to organisations that own and iterate on their processes.

The curve is conservative. It assumes efficiency gains plateau around placement #20. In practice, organisations with mature recruitment functions continue to find incremental improvements through analytics, process refinement, and pipeline network effects.

## 6. Infrastructure ROI Timeline

Infrastructure is not a day-one cost reduction. It is a 90-day investment with a defined return trajectory.

**Month 1-3: Setup and Calibration**
The infrastructure model is most expensive per placement during the initial period. Processes are being established, rubrics are being created, and the pipeline is being built. Cost per hire may exceed the agency benchmark during this phase.

**Month 4-6: Refinement**
Processes are refined based on first-placement data. Screening criteria are calibrated. Pipeline begins to accumulate candidates from previous searches. Cost per hire begins to decline.

**Month 7-12: Compound Efficiency**
The infrastructure model begins to outperform the agency model on cost per hire. Process maturity is building. Role-specific playbooks are established. Pipeline depth enables faster time-to-fill for subsequent roles.

**Year 2+: Mature Operations**
The cost per hire continues to decline (though at a slower rate). The organisation has a fully owned recruitment function with process documentation, candidate data, screening rubrics, and institutional hiring knowledge. The function operates independently of external providers.

## 7. Implications for Budget Planning

### Fixed vs. Variable Cost Structure

The transition from agency to infrastructure recruitment mirrors the transition from outsourced to owned operations in other business functions. The financial planning implications are identical:

- **Forecasting accuracy improves.** Fixed costs are predictable. Variable costs are dependent on hiring timing, salary conditions, and agency availability.
- **Budget variance decreases.** No surprise invoices when a critical role commands a higher salary than budgeted.
- **Capital allocation clarity.** The recruitment line item becomes a known operational expense, not a demand-driven variable.

### The Three-Year Budget Question

The relevant question for financial planning is not "what did we spend on recruitment this year?" but "what does our recruitment cost trajectory look like over the next three years, and is it improving?"

Under the agency model, the honest answer is that costs will increase in line with salary inflation, with no mechanism for efficiency improvement. Under infrastructure, costs are fixed and the cost per hire declines.

### Employer Cost Pressures (UK)

UK employers face additional cost pressure from National Insurance changes effective April 2025 -- the employer NI rate increasing to 15 percent on salaries above GBP 5,000 (HM Government, 2024). This increases the total cost of employment, which in turn increases the base salary that agency percentage fees are calculated against. Fixed infrastructure costs are insulated from this secondary escalation.
