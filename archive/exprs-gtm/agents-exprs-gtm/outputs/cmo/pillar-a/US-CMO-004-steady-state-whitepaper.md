# The Steady-State Framework: Recruitment as Operational Infrastructure

**A whitepaper on transforming recruitment from volatile expense to predictable function**

*Published by Exprs.io | February 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

Most companies with 20 to 100 employees treat recruitment as a series of isolated transactions. Each hiring need triggers a new agency engagement, a new fee, and a fresh start -- regardless of how many times the organisation has hired before. This paper introduces the Steady-State Framework: the principle that recruitment should function as always-on operational infrastructure rather than a volatile, event-driven expense. Drawing on industry research from SHRM (2024), Bersin by Deloitte (2019), CIPD (2024), and McKinsey (2024), the framework demonstrates that organisations which build process-driven recruitment functions achieve measurable reductions in cost per hire, gain compound efficiency across placements, and insulate themselves from salary-driven cost escalation. The Steady-State concept does not argue against recruitment agencies in all circumstances. It argues that the default reliance on transactional placement for repeatable roles is a structural inefficiency that compounds over time.

---

## 1. The Problem: Recruitment as Volatile Expense

The prevailing model for scaling companies treats recruitment as a procurement event. When a position opens, the company engages an external agency, pays a percentage-based fee upon successful placement, and the engagement ends. This is functionally equivalent to renting equipment for each project rather than investing in owned machinery.

The numbers describe the scope of this pattern clearly. Agency placement fees typically range from 15 to 25 percent of the new hire's first-year salary (SHRM, 2024). For a UK-based company making five placements annually at a blended median salary of GBP 55,000 (Payscale, 2025), the annual agency cost ranges from GBP 45,000 to GBP 65,000. In the US market, the equivalent figure for five placements at $120,000 blended median is $90,000 to $150,000.

These are direct costs only. The transactional model carries three additional cost categories that do not appear on the agency invoice.

### 1.1 Cost Unpredictability

Agency fees scale linearly with salary inflation. UK wage growth reached 4.5 percent in late 2025 (ONS, 2025). A five percent annual salary increase on a GBP 55,000 role raises the agency fee from GBP 11,000 to GBP 12,730 within three years -- an automatic 15.7 percent increase in recruitment cost with no corresponding improvement in process or efficiency. Fixed-cost infrastructure models are insulated from this escalation.

### 1.2 Knowledge Loss

Each agency placement is a standalone transaction. The screening criteria developed during the search, the candidate pipeline data generated, the role-specific playbooks refined through interviewing -- all of this intellectual property remains with the agency or evaporates entirely. The CIPD reports the average UK cost per hire at GBP 6,125, rising to GBP 19,000 for manager-level positions (CIPD, 2024). These figures represent the visible cost. Research on employee replacement indicates that 48 percent of organisations report losing institutional knowledge with each personnel transition (SHRM, 2022). The same dynamic applies to the recruitment process itself: every engagement starts from zero.

### 1.3 Structural Dependency

Without owned recruitment processes, a company has no alternative to its current agency relationship. Switching to a different agency resets institutional knowledge again. There is no competitive leverage in the relationship because there is no internal capability to fall back on. This dependency is not a failure of any individual agency. It is a structural property of the transactional model.

## 2. The Concept: Steady-State Infrastructure

The Steady-State Framework proposes that recruitment should operate as a predictable, always-on organisational function -- similar to how companies treat IT infrastructure, financial systems, or customer support operations.

Three properties define the Steady-State model:

### 2.1 Predictable Cost

A fixed operational expense replaces a variable, percentage-based fee structure. The organisation's recruitment budget becomes a forecastable line item rather than a figure that fluctuates with salary market conditions, hiring velocity, and agency pricing power. For financial planning purposes, this transforms recruitment from a demand-driven variable cost into a capacity-driven fixed cost -- the same transition that cloud computing introduced to IT infrastructure.

### 2.2 Process Ownership

All recruitment intellectual property -- job description templates, candidate screening rubrics, interview calibration data, pipeline databases, process documentation -- is owned by the organisation from the first day of operation. Nothing leaves when an engagement ends because the engagement does not end. The function is continuous.

### 2.3 Compound Efficiency

Each placement improves the next. This is the defining economic advantage of infrastructure over transactions. Bersin by Deloitte's Talent Acquisition Maturity Model provides the research foundation: organisations at Level 4 maturity (process-driven, analytics-enabled) perform 2.6 times higher across talent acquisition metrics than organisations at Level 1 (reactive, ad-hoc) (Bersin by Deloitte, 2019). High-maturity organisations demonstrate 18 percent higher revenue and 30 percent greater profitability than their low-maturity counterparts.

The compound efficiency manifests through four specific mechanisms:

- **Job description refinement.** Each hiring cycle improves the precision of role specifications, reducing misaligned applications and screening time.
- **Screening rubric calibration.** Interview evaluation criteria become more predictive with each data point, improving quality-of-hire metrics.
- **Pipeline accumulation.** Candidates not selected for one role remain in an owned database for future opportunities, reducing time-to-fill for subsequent positions.
- **Process optimisation.** Hiring manager feedback loops, offer acceptance analytics, and onboarding data create a continuous improvement system.

McKinsey's workforce research supports this trajectory, indicating that organisations using structured, skills-based hiring approaches achieve a 15 percent reduction in hiring timelines (McKinsey, 2024). This efficiency gain is only available to organisations that own and iterate on their processes.

## 3. The Shift: From Event to Function

The distinction between the transactional model and the Steady-State model is not one of quality or intent. Agencies employ skilled recruiters and deliver placement outcomes. The distinction is structural.

| Dimension | Transactional Model | Steady-State Model |
|-----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Cost behaviour | Variable: scales with salary and volume | Fixed: predictable regardless of conditions |
| Knowledge trajectory | Resets to zero per engagement | Compounds with each placement |
| Process ownership | External (agency retains) | Internal (organisation owns) |
| Dependency | High: no internal alternative | Low: function operates independently |
| Efficiency over time | Flat: placement #10 costs the same as #1 | Declining: each placement reduces marginal cost |
| Budget forecasting | Difficult: dependent on hiring timing and salary | Straightforward: fixed monthly line item |

This is not an argument that all agency relationships should end. It is an argument that the default should shift. For organisations hiring five or more people per year in repeatable role categories, the economics of infrastructure consistently outperform the economics of transaction.

## 4. The Maturity Argument: Why Process Ownership Compounds

The concept of compound efficiency requires evidence beyond intuition. Bersin by Deloitte's research across thousands of organisations provides the empirical foundation (Bersin by Deloitte, 2019).

Their Talent Acquisition Maturity Model defines four levels:

- **Level 1 -- Reactive.** Ad-hoc recruitment, no standardised processes, reliance on external agencies for most functions.
- **Level 2 -- Standardised.** Basic processes exist but are inconsistently applied. Some internal capability but heavy agency dependency.
- **Level 3 -- Strategic.** Recruitment is treated as a business function with defined metrics, processes, and technology support.
- **Level 4 -- Optimised.** Data-driven, analytics-enabled, continuous improvement embedded in the function.

The performance differential is not marginal. Level 4 organisations outperform Level 1 organisations by a factor of 2.6 across all talent acquisition performance drivers (Bersin by Deloitte, 2019). This includes time-to-fill, quality of hire, cost per hire, and hiring manager satisfaction.

The critical insight is that the agency model, by structural design, maintains clients at Level 1. Each engagement is reactive and does not advance the organisation's maturity. An infrastructure model moves the organisation from Level 1 toward Level 3 or 4 -- and the efficiency gains compound with each step.

## 5. When Steady-State Applies -- and When It Does Not

The Steady-State Framework is not universal. It is diagnostic. The appropriate model depends on the organisation's growth trajectory and role composition.

**Infrastructure is compelling at 5+ repeatable hires per year.** At this volume, the fixed cost of infrastructure is at or below the variable cost of agencies, and compound efficiency begins to accumulate.

**Infrastructure is marginal at 3-4 hires per year.** The direct cost comparison is approximately even, but infrastructure delivers process ownership and salary escalation protection that the agency model does not.

**Agencies may be more appropriate at 1-2 hires per year.** At very low volume, the overhead of maintaining infrastructure may not be justified -- unless the organisation plans to grow beyond this threshold within 12 to 18 months.

**Specialist placements remain valid regardless of volume.** Some roles -- senior leadership, niche technical specialists, board-level appointments -- require specialist market knowledge and deep network access that a generalist infrastructure model does not replicate. The Surgical vs. Systemic Map (a companion framework) provides the diagnostic tool for this distinction.

The question is not "infrastructure or agencies." The question is "which roles benefit from infrastructure, and which genuinely require specialist placement?"

## 6. Culture Debt: The Hidden Cost of Not Building Capability

Technical debt is a well-understood concept in software engineering: the accumulated cost of choosing expedient solutions over sustainable ones. Research from MIT Sloan Management Review and Forrester has established that technical debt affects over 90 percent of organisations and is cited as the single largest challenge by CTOs heading into recent years (MIT Sloan, 2023; Forrester, 2024).

The Steady-State Framework introduces an analogous concept: **culture debt in people operations.** When organisations repeatedly default to transactional recruitment rather than building internal capability, they accumulate operational debt that compounds:

- **Process debt:** No standardised hiring methodology exists. Each search reinvents the approach.
- **Knowledge debt:** Institutional understanding of what constitutes a successful hire in this organisation -- the tacit knowledge that improves screening quality -- never develops.
- **Pipeline debt:** No owned candidate database accumulates. Each search starts from a cold market.
- **Dependency debt:** The organisation becomes structurally unable to recruit without external support, even for straightforward roles.

This debt is invisible on the balance sheet. It manifests as slower time-to-fill, higher cost per hire that never declines, and a persistent inability to forecast or control recruitment outcomes.

## 7. Implications for Financial Planning

For the CFO or financial director evaluating recruitment operations, the Steady-State Framework offers a reframing of the budget conversation.

The traditional question is: "How much did we spend on recruitment this year?" The better question is: "What does our recruitment cost trajectory look like over three years, and is it improving?"

Under the agency model, the honest answer is that costs will increase in line with salary inflation, with no mechanism for efficiency gain. Under the infrastructure model, costs are fixed and the cost per hire declines with each placement.

This is the same calculation that justified the transition from on-premises servers to cloud infrastructure, from outsourced bookkeeping to internal finance functions, and from contracted customer support to owned operations. In each case, the economics reached a tipping point where owned infrastructure outperformed rented capacity -- not because renting was wrong, but because the organisation's volume and repeatability justified the investment.

Recruitment reaches this tipping point at a lower threshold than most organisations expect.
