## 1. Research Summary

### The Problem: Recruitment as Volatile Expense

The dominant model for companies with 20-100 employees treats recruitment as a transactional, variable-cost event. Each hire triggers a new agency engagement, a new fee (typically 15-25% of first-year salary, per SHRM benchmarking data) (SHRM, 2024), and a full reset of process knowledge. The result is a boom-bust cycle: intense activity during growth periods, total dormancy between them, and zero compound efficiency across placements.

This volatility creates three measurable consequences:

1. **Cost unpredictability.** Agency fees scale linearly with salary inflation. A 10% rise in market salaries produces a 10% rise in recruitment costs, with no efficiency offset. For a company making 5 placements annually at blended median UK salaries (GBP 45K-65K range) (Payscale, 2025), agency fees range from GBP 45K-65K per year -- a variable line item that resists forecasting.

2. **Knowledge loss.** Each agency placement is a standalone transaction. Screening criteria, candidate pipeline data, role-specific playbooks, and interview calibration reset to zero after the placement concludes. Research from SHRM and Gallup indicates that 48% of organisations lose institutional knowledge with each personnel departure (SHRM, 2022) -- the same principle applies to the recruitment process itself when it is outsourced on a per-transaction basis.

3. **Dependency lock-in.** Without owned processes, the company becomes structurally dependent on external providers. There is no internal capability that improves over time. The tenth placement costs the same as the first. This contradicts fundamental principles of operational maturity, where process-driven functions should demonstrate declining marginal costs through learning-curve effects.

### The Positioning Angle: Infrastructure, Not Service

The Steady-State Framework reframes recruitment from a volatile expense into a predictable operational function -- analogous to how companies treat IT infrastructure, accounting systems, or cloud computing. The core proposition is not "cheaper recruitment" but "recruitment as infrastructure": always-on, owned, and improving.

This positions against the status quo (agency dependency) without attacking agencies directly. The framing is diagnostic: "Here is the cost structure you currently operate within. Here is what infrastructure looks like instead. The maths determines which model fits your growth trajectory."

### Why Current Agencies Fail at Scale

The agency model is optimised for placement volume, not client capability building. This is a structural incentive misalignment, not a quality critique. Agencies earn revenue per placement; their economic incentive is to maximise placements, not to make the client self-sufficient.

Research from Bersin by Deloitte's Talent Acquisition Maturity Model shows that organisations at Level 4 maturity (process-driven, analytics-enabled) perform 2.6x higher across talent acquisition performance metrics than Level 1 organisations (reactive, ad-hoc) (Bersin by Deloitte, 2019). The agency model, by design, keeps clients at Level 1 -- each engagement is reactive and does not build toward process maturity.

Additionally, McKinsey's workforce research indicates organisations using structured, skills-based hiring approaches reduced hiring timelines by an average of 15% (McKinsey, 2024). This efficiency gain is only available to organisations that own and iterate on their recruitment processes -- not those outsourcing them transactionally.
