## Objection 1: "We only hire 2-3 people a year. Infrastructure is overkill."

**Persona:** Founder (small team, early growth)
**Pillar challenged:** [C] Pragmatic Costing, [A] Steady-State

**Root concern:** The fixed cost does not justify the hiring volume.

**CMO-approved response:**
At 2-3 placements per year, the direct cost comparison is approximately even -- agencies at GBP 22K-33K versus infrastructure at GBP 36K (see Pragmatic Costing whitepaper, Section 3, Scenario 1). The relevant question is growth trajectory. If the organisation plans to stay at 2-3 placements permanently, agencies are a reasonable model. If growth to 5+ placements is anticipated within 12-18 months, infrastructure begins compounding efficiency now -- and the cost per hire declines with each placement. Bersin by Deloitte (2019) research on the Talent Acquisition Maturity Model supports this compound efficiency trajectory. The honest answer: at 2-3 hires with no growth planned, agencies may be the right choice. That consultative honesty builds trust.

**Whitepaper reference:** Pragmatic Costing, Sections 3 and 5.
**Source:** Bersin, J. (2019) *Talent Acquisition Maturity Model*. Available at: https://www.bersin.com/practice/detail.aspx?id=19844 [Accessed: 13 February 2026].

## Objection 2: "Our agency finds great people. Why would we change something that works?"

**Persona:** Founder or HR Director (satisfied with current provider)
**Pillar challenged:** [A] Steady-State, [B] Surgical vs. Systemic

**Root concern:** Switching cost and disruption risk when the current model delivers acceptable outcomes.

**CMO-approved response:**
The question is not whether the agency finds good people. Many do. The question is what happens to the process knowledge after each placement. When the agency finishes, the screening criteria, candidate pipeline, and role-specific playbooks leave with them. The next time the company hires for a similar role, the process starts from zero. Bersin by Deloitte (2019) data shows that process-driven recruitment functions perform 2.6x higher than reactive ones. Agencies deliver good individual outcomes. Infrastructure delivers outcomes that improve with each cycle. Both can coexist -- the Surgical vs. Systemic framework shows when each approach adds the most value.

**Whitepaper reference:** Steady-State, Section 4; Surgical vs. Systemic, Section 1.3.
**Source:** Bersin, J. (2019) *Talent Acquisition Maturity Model*. Available at: https://www.bersin.com/practice/detail.aspx?id=19844 [Accessed: 13 February 2026].

## Objection 3: "We already have an internal recruiter. We do not need another recruitment tool."

**Persona:** Founder or HR Director
**Pillar challenged:** [A] Steady-State

**Root concern:** Perceived overlap between an internal hire and an infrastructure system.

**CMO-approved response:**
An internal recruiter is a person. Infrastructure is the system they operate within. Job description templates that improve with each use. Screening rubrics that calibrate over time. A candidate pipeline that accumulates rather than resetting. Process documentation that survives staff turnover. Your recruiter spends less time on administrative tasks, sourcing from scratch, and process design -- and more time on the conversations that actually close candidates. Infrastructure amplifies what your recruiter already does. It does not replace them.

**Whitepaper reference:** Steady-State, Sections 2.2 and 2.3; Brand Voice Guide, Sample 4.

## Objection 4: "GBP 36K per year is expensive for a company our size."

**Persona:** CFO or Finance Director
**Pillar challenged:** [C] Pragmatic Costing

**Root concern:** Sticker shock on the fixed cost without understanding the comparative economics.

**CMO-approved response:**
The GBP 36K figure needs context. At 5 placements per year with agencies at 20% of a GBP 55K median salary (Payscale, 2025), the agency cost is GBP 55K annually. Over three years with salary escalation (ONS, 2025), that reaches GBP 173K. Infrastructure over the same period: GBP 108K. The three-year difference is GBP 65K (Pragmatic Costing whitepaper, Section 3, Scenario 2). Beyond direct cost, infrastructure delivers process ownership, compound efficiency, and insulation from salary-driven fee escalation. The question for the CFO is not "is GBP 36K expensive?" but "what does the three-year recruitment cost trajectory look like under each model?"

**Whitepaper reference:** Pragmatic Costing, Sections 3 and 4.
**Sources:** Payscale (2025) *UK Salary Report*. Available at: https://www.payscale.com/research/UK/Country=United_Kingdom/Salary [Accessed: 13 February 2026]. ONS (2025) *Average Weekly Earnings*. Available at: https://www.ons.gov.uk/employmentandlabourmarket/peopleinwork/earningsandworkinghours/bulletins/averageweeklyearningsingreatbritain/latest [Accessed: 13 February 2026].

## Objection 5: "Can we try it for one month to see if it works?"

**Persona:** Founder (risk-averse, wants proof before commitment)
**Pillar challenged:** [A] Steady-State, [C] Pragmatic Costing

**Root concern:** Unwillingness to commit without evidence of value.

**CMO-approved response:**
Infrastructure needs 90 days to compound. Month one is setup and calibration -- building processes, defining rubrics, establishing the pipeline. Month two is refinement -- the first placements generate data that improves the process. Month three is where compound efficiency becomes visible -- the cost per hire begins to decline, and the process demonstrably improves. Evaluating infrastructure in one month is equivalent to judging a cloud migration by the first week of implementation. The recommendation is a 90-day commitment with clear milestones at each stage: process documentation complete (month 1), first placement with feedback loop (month 2), measurable efficiency improvement (month 3).

**Whitepaper reference:** Pragmatic Costing, Section 6 (Infrastructure ROI Timeline).

## Objection 6: "Agencies have deeper networks for hard-to-fill roles."

**Persona:** CTO or VP Engineering (concerned about specialist talent)
**Pillar challenged:** [B] Surgical vs. Systemic

**Root concern:** Fear that infrastructure cannot source niche technical talent.

**CMO-approved response:**
This is correct for genuinely specialist roles. A CISO search, a VP Engineering hire, a niche ML researcher -- these are surgical placements where deep network access and specialist market knowledge add clear value. The Surgical vs. Systemic Map addresses this directly. Most companies at the 20-100 employee stage find that 70-85% of their roles are systemic -- repeatable, well-defined, accessible through standard channels. The remaining 15-30% are genuinely surgical. The argument is not to replace agencies entirely. It is to stop paying surgical rates for systemic work, and to reserve specialist agency budget for the roles that genuinely need it. SHRM (2022) data on replacement costs confirms that leadership wrong-hire costs can reach 200% of annual salary, justifying specialist placement for those roles.

**Whitepaper reference:** Surgical vs. Systemic, Sections 1.1 and 4 (Scenario walkthroughs).
**Source:** SHRM (2022) *Retaining Talent*. Available at: https://www.shrm.org/topics-tools/tools/toolkits/managing-employee-turnover [Accessed: 13 February 2026].

## Objection 7: "What happens if we scale faster than expected? Does the pricing change?"

**Persona:** Founder or CFO (planning for rapid growth)
**Pillar challenged:** [C] Pragmatic Costing

**Root concern:** Hidden costs or pricing changes at scale.

**CMO-approved response:**
The cost remains fixed. That is the structural point of infrastructure. Under the agency model, doubling hiring volume doubles the cost. Under infrastructure, the cost stays the same. At 10 placements per year, the three-year agency cost reaches GBP 346K versus GBP 108K for infrastructure (Pragmatic Costing whitepaper, Section 3, Scenario 3). At 20 placements, it is GBP 693K versus GBP 108K. Infrastructure becomes more cost-effective as volume increases, not less. The cost per hire declines with each placement through compound efficiency (Bersin by Deloitte, 2019), while the total cost remains flat.

**Whitepaper reference:** Pragmatic Costing, Sections 3 and 5.

## Objection 8: "How do I know the process knowledge is actually being captured? What if your system is just another vendor?"

**Persona:** CTO (sceptical of vendor claims)
**Pillar challenged:** [A] Steady-State

**Root concern:** Vendor dependency dressed as infrastructure.

**CMO-approved response:**
A fair challenge. The distinction is ownership. With an agency, the screening criteria, pipeline data, and process documentation are not yours -- they leave when the engagement ends. With infrastructure, every job description template, screening rubric, candidate database record, and process document belongs to the organisation from day one. If the infrastructure relationship ends, the organisation retains everything. Nothing leaves. This is the definition of infrastructure versus service: you own the asset. The Steady-State whitepaper (Section 2.2) details the specific knowledge assets that transfer.

**Whitepaper reference:** Steady-State, Sections 2.2 and 3.

## Objection 9: "The recruitment market is soft right now. We can get better agency rates."

**Persona:** CFO (opportunistic pricing)
**Pillar challenged:** [C] Pragmatic Costing

**Root concern:** Short-term market conditions favour negotiation over infrastructure investment.

**CMO-approved response:**
Market conditions fluctuate. Agency rates may be negotiable today. They were not 18 months ago, and they may not be in 12 months. The Pragmatic Costing framework addresses this: percentage-based fees are structurally tied to salary inflation regardless of competitive pressure on the agency margin (ONS, 2025). A 15% rate instead of 20% still compounds with salary escalation. And market softness does not address the three other cost layers: knowledge retention (SHRM, 2022), process ownership, and dependency. Infrastructure decisions should be based on three-year trajectories, not current spot pricing.

**Whitepaper reference:** Pragmatic Costing, Sections 2 and 4.
**Sources:** ONS (2025) *Average Weekly Earnings*. Available at: https://www.ons.gov.uk/employmentandlabourmarket/peopleinwork/earningsandworkinghours/bulletins/averageweeklyearningsingreatbritain/latest [Accessed: 13 February 2026]. SHRM (2022) *Retaining Talent*. Available at: https://www.shrm.org/topics-tools/tools/toolkits/managing-employee-turnover [Accessed: 13 February 2026].

## Objection 10: "We have tried RPO before and it did not work."

**Persona:** Founder (burned by previous outsourcing)
**Pillar challenged:** [A] Steady-State

**Root concern:** Previous negative experience with recruitment outsourcing.

**CMO-approved response:**
RPO (Recruitment Process Outsourcing) and infrastructure are structurally different. RPO is still outsourcing -- the process is managed by an external provider and the knowledge typically remains with them. Infrastructure is owned. The processes, data, rubrics, and pipeline belong to the organisation. If a previous RPO engagement failed to build internal capability, that is precisely the problem the Steady-State model addresses. The question is not "should we outsource recruitment?" but "should we own the recruitment function and invest in making it better over time?"

**Whitepaper reference:** Steady-State, Sections 2 and 3.
