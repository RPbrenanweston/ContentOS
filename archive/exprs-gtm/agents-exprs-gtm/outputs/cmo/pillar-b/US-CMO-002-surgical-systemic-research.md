## 1. Research Summary

### The Core Distinction

Not every hire requires the same approach. The fundamental diagnostic error most 20-100 employee companies make is applying one solution -- typically a recruitment agency -- to two fundamentally different problems.

**Surgical hires** are low-frequency, high-complexity placements where the role demands specialist market knowledge, a narrow candidate pool, or domain-specific screening expertise. Examples: a VP of Engineering for a Series B fintech, a Chief Information Security Officer, a regulatory compliance specialist. These roles benefit from specialist placement because the search is non-repeatable and the cost of a wrong hire is disproportionately high.

**Systemic hires** are repeatable, medium-frequency placements where the role type recurs across hiring cycles and the screening process can be standardised. Examples: software engineers, account managers, marketing coordinators, operations analysts. These roles benefit from infrastructure because the process compounds -- each placement improves job descriptions, screening rubrics, interview calibration, and pipeline depth.

The problem is not that agencies exist. The problem is that companies pay specialist (surgical) rates for work that is structurally systemic.

### The Diagnostic Framework

Drawing from procurement strategy research -- specifically Strategy&'s three-pillar make-vs-buy framework covering business strategy, risk, and economic factors (Strategy&, 2023) -- and operations management decision theory, the Surgical vs. Systemic Map uses five diagnostic questions to help founders self-assess:

**Question 1: Do you hire this role type more than twice per year?**
If yes, the role is likely systemic. Repeatable roles benefit from owned process infrastructure. If no, the role may be surgical -- a specialist placement is more cost-effective than building capability for a one-off search.

**Question 2: Can you describe the ideal candidate profile in specific, measurable terms?**
If yes, the screening criteria can be systematised into a rubric and improved over time. Infrastructure captures this learning. If no, the role requires exploratory, specialist sourcing -- a surgical approach.

**Question 3: Does this role exist in a well-defined talent market?**
If yes, the candidate pool is accessible through standard sourcing channels. Infrastructure works. If no, the role requires deep network access or niche market expertise -- surgical.

**Question 4: What is the cost of a wrong hire for this role?**
For systemic roles, the cost is significant but bounded (typically 50-200% of annual salary) (SHRM, 2022). For surgical roles -- particularly leadership positions -- the cost extends to strategic direction, team morale, and investor confidence. Higher wrong-hire cost favours surgical precision.

**Question 5: Is this a capability you need to build internally over time?**
If you will need to hire variations of this role repeatedly (e.g., "software engineer" evolving to "senior engineer" then "principal engineer"), the learning curve argument favours infrastructure. Each placement refines the process. If it is a one-off strategic hire, there is no learning curve to capture.

### Branching Logic Summary

```
Q1: Hire this role 2+ times/year?
  YES --> Likely SYSTEMIC (proceed to Q2)
  NO  --> Likely SURGICAL (proceed to Q4 for confirmation)

Q2: Can you describe ideal candidate in measurable terms?
  YES --> SYSTEMIC confirmed (infrastructure captures learning)
  NO  --> HYBRID (may need surgical sourcing, systemic process)

Q3: Well-defined talent market?
  YES --> SYSTEMIC (standard channels work)
  NO  --> SURGICAL (niche network required)

Q4: Cost of wrong hire?
  BOUNDED (50-200% salary) --> Supports SYSTEMIC approach
  STRATEGIC (direction, morale, investor) --> Supports SURGICAL approach

Q5: Need to build this capability long-term?
  YES --> SYSTEMIC (learning curve compounds)
  NO  --> SURGICAL (one-off, no learning to capture)
```

### Scenario Walkthroughs

**Scenario A: Series A startup (25 employees, hiring 3 engineers + 1 VP Eng)**
- Engineers (x3): Q1=YES, Q2=YES, Q3=YES --> SYSTEMIC. Build the process now; it pays off by hire #5.
- VP Engineering (x1): Q1=NO, Q2=PARTIALLY, Q4=STRATEGIC --> SURGICAL. Specialist placement justified.
- Diagnosis: 75% systemic, 25% surgical. The company is overpaying if all four roles go through agencies.

**Scenario B: Scaling company (60 employees, hiring 8 roles across engineering, sales, ops)**
- Engineering (x4): SYSTEMIC. Process from Scenario A compounds here.
- Sales (x2): Q1=YES, Q2=YES, Q3=YES --> SYSTEMIC.
- Ops Manager (x1): Q1=NO, Q2=YES, Q3=YES --> SYSTEMIC (even at low volume, the process applies broadly).
- Head of Data (x1): Q1=NO, Q2=PARTIALLY, Q3=NO, Q4=STRATEGIC --> SURGICAL.
- Diagnosis: 87.5% systemic. One specialist placement. Seven roles benefit from infrastructure.

**Scenario C: Rapid-growth company (90 employees, 15+ hires across all functions)**
- 12-13 roles: SYSTEMIC. High-volume, repeatable, well-defined markets.
- 2-3 roles: SURGICAL. Leadership, niche specialists, board-level hires.
- Diagnosis: 80-85% systemic. The agency model is structurally wrong for the majority of these hires.
