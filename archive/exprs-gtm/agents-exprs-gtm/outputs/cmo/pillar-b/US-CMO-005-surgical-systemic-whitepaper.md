# The Surgical vs. Systemic Map: A Diagnostic Framework for Recruitment Strategy

**How to determine when specialist placement adds value -- and when infrastructure is the right investment**

*Published by Exprs.io | February 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

Not every hire requires the same approach. Yet most companies with 20 to 100 employees apply a single solution -- the recruitment agency -- to two fundamentally different categories of hiring need. This paper introduces the Surgical vs. Systemic Map: a diagnostic framework that helps founders and operational leaders distinguish between roles that genuinely require specialist placement (surgical) and roles that benefit from owned recruitment infrastructure (systemic). Using five diagnostic questions and three scenario walkthroughs, this framework enables organisations to audit their current hiring spend and identify where they may be paying specialist rates for work that is structurally repeatable. The framework draws on procurement strategy research (Strategy&, 2023), talent acquisition maturity data (Bersin by Deloitte, 2019), and industry cost benchmarks (CIPD, 2024; SHRM, 2024). It is not anti-agency. It is pro-diagnosis.

## 1. Two Modes of Hiring -- One Default Solution

When a scaling company needs to fill a role, the typical response is to contact a recruitment agency. This is not irrational. Agencies offer speed, market access, and screening capability. For many roles, particularly at the senior or specialist end, this approach is entirely appropriate.

The problem arises when this same approach is applied uniformly across all roles. A VP of Engineering search and a fourth software engineer hire are fundamentally different problems. The first requires deep network access, nuanced market positioning, and candidate assessment that goes beyond technical screening. The second requires a well-defined candidate profile, a calibrated screening rubric, and a repeatable process -- the same process used for the first, second, and third software engineer.

Paying specialist placement fees for the second category is not a failure of agencies. It is a failure of diagnosis.

### 1.1 Defining Surgical

**Surgical hires** are low-frequency, high-complexity placements where:

- The role demands specialist market knowledge or deep network access
- The candidate pool is narrow and not accessible through standard channels
- The cost of a wrong hire extends beyond replacement expense to strategic direction, team morale, or investor confidence
- The organisation does not expect to hire for this specific role type again in the near term

Examples: Chief Technology Officer, VP of Engineering, Chief Information Security Officer, Head of Regulatory Compliance, specialist data scientist for a niche domain.

Surgical hires benefit from specialist placement because the search is non-repeatable and the stakes are disproportionately high. SHRM research indicates that wrong-hire costs for leadership positions can reach 200 percent of annual salary when factoring in strategic disruption, team impact, and re-recruitment (SHRM, 2022).

### 1.2 Defining Systemic

**Systemic hires** are repeatable, medium-to-high frequency placements where:

- The role type recurs across hiring cycles (same role, different person)
- The ideal candidate profile can be described in specific, measurable terms
- The talent market for this role is well-defined and accessible through standard sourcing channels
- The screening process can be standardised and improved over time

Examples: software engineers, account managers, marketing coordinators, operations analysts, customer success managers, project managers.

Systemic hires benefit from infrastructure because the process compounds. Each placement refines the job description, calibrates the screening rubric, deepens the candidate pipeline, and optimises the interview process. Bersin by Deloitte's Talent Acquisition Maturity Model demonstrates that process-driven organisations achieve 2.6 times higher performance across talent acquisition metrics than reactive ones (Bersin by Deloitte, 2019).

### 1.3 The Spectrum

Surgical and systemic are not binary categories. They occupy a spectrum. Some roles begin as surgical (the first data engineer at a company with no data team) and become systemic over time (the fifth data engineer, hired using a refined process). The diagnostic framework accounts for this evolution.

## 2. The Five Diagnostic Questions

Before engaging any recruitment provider or launching an internal search, the following five questions determine whether a role is surgical, systemic, or hybrid.

### Question 1: Do you hire this role type more than twice per year?

**If yes:** The role is likely systemic. Repeatable hiring demand means the process can be standardised, refined, and improved across cycles. Infrastructure captures this learning. Crunchbase data shows that VC-backed startups in the 20-100 employee range have a median hiring velocity of 8 placements per year (Crunchbase, 2024) -- most of which are repeatable role types.

**If no:** The role may be surgical. A one-off hire for a unique role does not generate enough process repetition to justify building infrastructure around it. Proceed to Question 4 for further assessment.

### Question 2: Can you describe the ideal candidate profile in specific, measurable terms?

**If yes:** The screening criteria can be systematised into a rubric. This rubric becomes an asset that improves with each use -- a form of intellectual property that infrastructure retains. Example: "3-5 years of Python experience, familiarity with AWS services, experience in a B2B SaaS environment."

**If no:** The role requires exploratory sourcing. The candidate profile is ambiguous, the market is ill-defined, or the role itself is still being scoped. This favours a surgical approach with a specialist who can advise on role definition alongside sourcing.

### Question 3: Does this role exist in a well-defined talent market?

**If yes:** The candidate pool is accessible through standard sourcing channels -- job boards, LinkedIn, professional communities, referral networks. Infrastructure works because sourcing is not the bottleneck.

**If no:** The role requires deep network access or niche market expertise. A specialist agency with established relationships in this market is likely to outperform a generalist approach. This is a surgical indicator.

### Question 4: What is the cost of a wrong hire for this role?

**If bounded (50-200% of annual salary):** The SHRM-benchmarked replacement cost is significant but manageable (SHRM, 2022). This supports a systemic approach where the process improves over time and wrong-hire probability decreases with each hiring cycle.

**If strategic (direction, morale, investor confidence):** The wrong-hire cost extends well beyond the financial replacement figure. C-suite, VP-level, and critical leadership roles carry strategic risk that justifies the premium of specialist placement. A surgical approach with deep reference checking and market expertise reduces this risk.

### Question 5: Is this a capability you need to build internally over time?

**If yes:** You will hire variations of this role repeatedly. Software engineer today becomes senior engineer in six months and principal engineer next year. The learning curve from each hire compounds into a recruitment capability. Infrastructure captures this trajectory. Agencies do not.

**If no:** This is a one-off strategic hire. There is no learning curve to capture and no future demand to amortise the process investment against. A surgical approach is appropriate.

## 4. Scenario Walkthroughs

### Scenario A: Series A Startup (25 Employees, 4 Hires Planned)

**Roles:** 3 software engineers, 1 VP of Engineering

| Role | Q1 | Q2 | Q3 | Q4 | Q5 | Diagnosis |
|------|----|----|----|----|----|----|
| Software Engineer (x3) | YES (3/yr) | YES | YES | Bounded | YES | **SYSTEMIC** |
| VP Engineering (x1) | NO | Partial | Partial | Strategic | NO | **SURGICAL** |

**Analysis:** 75% of this company's hiring demand is systemic. The engineering roles recur, have measurable profiles, exist in a well-defined market, and represent a capability the company needs to build. The VP Engineering search is genuinely surgical: non-repeatable, strategically critical, requiring deep network access.

**Cost implication:** At 20% agency fee on GBP 55K median engineering salary (Payscale, 2025), the three systemic hires cost GBP 33,000 through agencies. Through infrastructure, these placements build a process that compounds for future engineering hires. The VP Engineering placement at GBP 18,000-25,000 (higher salary, higher fee) is appropriately surgical.

### Scenario B: Scaling Company (60 Employees, 8 Hires Planned)

**Roles:** 4 engineers, 2 sales executives, 1 operations manager, 1 Head of Data

| Role | Q1 | Q2 | Q3 | Q4 | Q5 | Diagnosis |
|------|----|----|----|----|----|----|
| Engineers (x4) | YES | YES | YES | Bounded | YES | **SYSTEMIC** |
| Sales Executives (x2) | YES | YES | YES | Bounded | YES | **SYSTEMIC** |
| Operations Manager (x1) | NO | YES | YES | Bounded | YES | **SYSTEMIC** |
| Head of Data (x1) | NO | Partial | NO | Strategic | NO | **SURGICAL** |

**Analysis:** 87.5% systemic. Seven of eight roles benefit from infrastructure. One role (Head of Data) is genuinely surgical: niche market, ambiguous profile (the company is building this function for the first time), and strategically significant.

**Cost implication:** Seven agency placements at 20% of blended GBP 50K median is GBP 70,000. Through infrastructure, these seven placements build a multi-function recruitment capability that serves engineering, sales, and operations hiring. The single surgical placement is handled by a specialist.

## 5. The Cost of Misdiagnosis

When surgical pricing is applied to systemic roles, the financial consequence is straightforward to quantify. But the operational consequence is more significant.

**Financial cost:** An organisation making 8 systemic hires per year through agencies at 20% of GBP 50K median salary spends GBP 80,000 annually. Over three years with 5% salary inflation (ONS, 2025): GBP 252,000.

**Operational cost:** Zero process improvement. Zero compound efficiency. Zero pipeline accumulation. The ninth hire costs the same as the first. The organisation has paid GBP 252,000 and owns nothing.

The misdiagnosis is not "agencies are expensive." The misdiagnosis is "we are using a specialist tool for a standard function." Infrastructure converts that same investment into owned capability with declining marginal cost.

## 6. How to Start: The Role Audit

The practical application of this framework begins with a role audit.

**Step 1: List every hire made in the past 12 months and every anticipated hire in the next 12 months.**

**Step 2: Run each role through the five diagnostic questions.** Mark each as surgical, systemic, or hybrid.

**Step 3: Calculate the percentage split.** Industry data suggests that most 20-100 employee companies will find 70-85% of their roles are systemic.

**Step 4: For systemic roles, evaluate infrastructure options.** What would a fixed-cost recruitment function look like for these roles? What is the 12-month TCO comparison?

**Step 5: For surgical roles, retain specialist agency relationships.** These are the roles where specialist placement genuinely adds value. Protecting this budget for surgical work, rather than diluting it across systemic roles, improves outcomes for the hires that matter most.

This is not an all-or-nothing transition. It is a diagnostic reallocation.
