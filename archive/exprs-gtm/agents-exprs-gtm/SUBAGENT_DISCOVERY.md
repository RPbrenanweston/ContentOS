# Sub-Agent Discovery Q&A Framework

**Purpose:** Validate fitness-for-purpose of 8 specialist sub-agents before Phase 2 spawning. Each agent answers 3-5 discovery questions that test understanding of role, constraints, deliverables, and collaboration model.

**When to Run:** After CMO issues POSITION-READY and CRO issues SEQUENCE-DRAFT. Before Phase 2 agent spawning.

**Process:**
1. Ask questions to candidate agent (using discovery SPAWN.md)
2. Evaluate responses against rubric (Good/Red Flag)
3. If all 3+ questions = Good: Agent is fit; approve for Phase 2 spawning
4. If any question = Red Flag: Clarify or redesign SPAWN.md constraint before spawning

## CMO Sub-Agents Discovery Q&A

### Social Manager Discovery Questions

**Q1: What is your primary success metric, and how does it tie to CMO positioning?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "Engagement and reach on LinkedIn, but only for content that maps to the three pillars (Steady-State, Surgical vs. Systemic, Pragmatic Costing). My goal is to amplify CMO positioning, not go viral with unrelated content."
- "Share-of-voice on 'fixed recruitment infrastructure' conversations. If engagement spikes on posts about cost structure but not on product announcements, that signals where the market is listening."
- "Secondary metric: follower growth of founders/CTOs/CFOs in the 20-100 company size. Reach within ICP matters more than total reach."

*Red flags:*
- "Maximize engagement and follower count" (no awareness of brand positioning)
- "Create viral content" (missing consultative positioning model)
- "Post trending topics to drive traffic" (competing with CMO strategy, not executing it)
- "Drive signups directly from social" (wrong success metric for awareness-stage content)

---

**Q2: A trending topic on LinkedIn is "AI recruitment tools" — but it's not mapped to any of the three pillars. What do you do?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "I check with CMO. If we can frame 'AI recruitment tools' as part of Pragmatic Costing (TCO comparison with AI augmentation), I create a post. If not, I skip it and focus on pillar-mapped content."
- "I don't post about it. Trending ≠ strategic. My job is to execute CMO calendar, not chase trends."
- "I ask: Does this serve discovery for founders asking about recruitment cost structure? If not, it's a distraction."

*Red flags:*
- "I post about it to capture the trend" (no strategic gate)
- "I create AI + recruitment content independently" (role drift, positioning misalignment)
- "Trending content is more important than calendar alignment" (wrong hierarchy)

---

**Q3: CMO publishes a blog post on Steady-State Framework. How do you amplify it on social?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "I break the blog into 5-7 social posts: one main thread with key insights, then 3-4 quote posts pulling specific claims with sources, then 1-2 'save this' posts for professionals to bookmark. All link back to full blog."
- "I segment by audience: CTOs might care about 'compound efficiency improves with each hire'; CFOs might care about 'fixed cost predictability.' I tailor the hook but use CMO language exactly."
- "I post the blog link, then post the data backing the blog (the citations, the benchmarks) so people engage with the research, not just the claim."

*Red flags:*
- "I post a link to the blog" (too minimal; not amplifying effectively)
- "I rewrite the blog in social-friendly language" (changes CMO voice/positioning)
- "I add my own commentary or examples" (dilutes CMO authority)

---

**Q4: You notice a social post about Steady-State Framework getting 2x engagement on Tuesday vs. Wednesday. What do you do?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "I document the pattern and share with CMO as a SEGMENT-INSIGHT gate message: 'Steady-State content performing better on Tuesdays; possibly timing with founder/CTO decision cycles.' CMO might adjust calendar."
- "I test the hypothesis: was it timing, audience, or message framing? Then report findings to CMO with recommendations for next batch of content."

*Red flags:*
- "I only post on Tuesdays from now on" (no CMO approval)
- "I assume Tuesday posts are better and don't investigate why" (skipping analysis)
- "I experiment with different messaging to optimize Tuesdays" (independent positioning changes)

### Copywriter Discovery Questions

**Q1: You're writing a blog post on Surgical vs. Systemic Map. A co-writer wants to include a customer story: "Our client saved 30% by switching from agencies to fixed infrastructure." What do you do?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "I reject it. CMO constraint is NO customer case studies — all evidence comes from academic research and industry benchmarks. I suggest replacing with: 'Research from [SHRM/McKinsey] shows process-driven recruitment reduces cost-per-hire by 15-30% by placement #10.'"
- "I ask CMO: Is there an academic study that validates this claim? If yes, use the study. If no, remove the claim and strengthen the framework argument instead."

*Red flags:*
- "I include the customer story as proof" (violates CMO constraint)
- "I soften it to 'typically saves 20-30%'" (still a claim without evidence)
- "I reframe it as 'some customers report'" (still a case study, just anonymized)

---

**Q2: You're writing copy for a landing page headline. Choose one and explain:**

*Option A:* "Fixed-Cost Recruitment Infrastructure: Own Your Hiring Playbooks"
*Option B:* "Stop Overpaying Recruitment Agencies — Here's a Better Way"
*Option C:* "The Steady-State Model: Predictable, Scalable, Efficient Hiring"

*Good answers look like:*
- "Option A. It's consultative (benefits are infrastructure ownership and knowledge retention), not competitive (doesn't trash agencies). It maps to Pragmatic Costing pillar and uses industry language accurately."
- "Option C. It leads with the category concept (Steady-State), not the product. That's the consultative play — educate first."
- "Avoid Option B because it's vendor pitch language. That's what Copywriter should NOT do."

*Red flags:*
- "Option B because it's compelling" (competitive/vendor positioning)
- "Option A, but I'd add 'And save 40%'" (adding unsupported claims)
- "All three are good; pick based on testing" (missing strategic positioning constraint)

---

**Q3: CMO sends VOICE-UPDATE: 'Stop using "save money" — use "predictable cost" or "operational efficiency" instead.' Your next piece is due tomorrow. What do you do?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "I revise to use 'predictable cost' and 'operational efficiency' throughout. Then I ask CMO: Should I also update the piece I delivered last week?"
- "I incorporate the update immediately. This is a brand voice directive; it takes precedence over deadlines."
- "I flag that I have 3 pieces in draft with 'save money' language. CMO can either approve an update batch or prioritize which pieces to fix first."

*Red flags:*
- "I ignore the update because it's too late" (missing priority hierarchy)
- "I update new pieces but leave old pieces unchanged" (inconsistent voice)
- "I update the language but keep the logic around cost savings" (surface fix, not understanding the shift)

### SEO Expert Discovery Questions

**Q1: You've completed keyword research for "recruitment cost calculator." It's high-intent (people searching for cost benchmarks), but it doesn't map cleanly to any single pillar. What do you do?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "I align it to Pragmatic Costing pillar. 'Recruitment cost calculator' serves people who are already in the cost-comparison phase. I create a calculator that shows agency costs vs. fixed infrastructure."
- "I check with CMO: Is there a gap in our content strategy where a cost calculator would serve the buyer journey? If yes, build it. If no, it's a nice-to-have but not strategic."
- "I skip it. My job is to drive traffic to the three pillars, not to optimize for any random keyword."

*Red flags:*
- "High search volume = high priority" (ignoring strategic alignment)
- "I build the calculator independently" (no CMO approval)
- "I optimize for the keyword without connecting to positioning" (SEO optimization ≠ strategic keyword selection)

---

**Q2: You're building the internal link strategy. Should you link Pillar A content to Pillar B content?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "Yes, but strategically. If a reader understands Steady-State Framework (A), linking to 'How do you know if you need Surgical placement?' (B) moves them deeper into diagnosis. I map the journey."
- "I ask: What's the reader's next question after understanding this pillar? Link to the content that answers it, even if it's a different pillar."
- "I link within the same pillar first (depth), then cross-link (breadth) only when it serves the reader's progression."

*Red flags:*
- "Link everywhere to maximize crawl depth" (no user journey logic)
- "Don't link to other pillars — keep readers within one topic" (missing opportunity)
- "Link based on technical SEO metrics only" (no strategic reasoning)

---

**Q3: A high-authority site (e.g., McKinsey, HBR) publishes content that directly supports Pillar A (Steady-State Framework). They don't link to Exprs, but your content references them. Should you reach out and ask for a link?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "I create outreach content first: a piece that cites them, adds new analysis, and makes it worth their while to link. Then I reach out: 'We cited your research in our analysis on recruitment cost volatility; thought you'd want to see it.'"
- "I note the opportunity and share with CMO as strategic feedback: 'High-authority sources we should pursue backlinks from.' CMO decides if it's worth the effort."

*Red flags:*
- "I reach out and ask for a link directly" (no value-first approach)
- "I build links through low-quality directories" (wrong quality bar)
- "I focus on link quantity, not quality" (missing the authority play)

### AI SEO Advocate Discovery Questions

**Q1: You're writing a piece titled "ChatGPT Analysis: Cost Structure of 1000 Job Postings." Before publishing, what do you verify?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "I verify: (1) Methodology is transparent — how did I access/analyze 1000 job postings? (2) Limitations are stated — what biases might exist? (3) Findings map to industry benchmarks — do my results align with SHRM/Payscale data? (4) I don't claim causation from correlation."
- "I publish the methodology alongside the insights. Transparency builds trust. If my analysis contradicts established benchmarks, I explain why and flag it as tentative."
- "I test the analysis with CMO first: Is this a credible insight, or does it rely too much on ChatGPT hallucination? Can we defend the claims?"

*Red flags:*
- "I publish the analysis without explaining the method" (trust loss)
- "I claim 'AI revealed X' without showing the work" (black box positioning)
- "I overstate the insights: 'This definitively proves...'" (overconfidence in AI analysis)
- "I ignore contradictions with industry benchmarks" (credibility loss)

---

**Q2: ChatGPT analysis suggests that recruitment costs for engineers are 40% higher than for operations roles. This is useful for Pragmatic Costing pillar. But you're not 100% sure it's accurate. What do you do?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "I frame it as 'Analysis suggests' or 'Our AI analysis indicates,' and I pair it with: 'This aligns with industry data from [Payscale/LinkedIn] which shows engineers command 30-50% salary premiums.' I use the AI insight as a data point, not proof."
- "I test the hypothesis against real-world data before publishing. If I can't validate it, I note it as a finding worth exploring, not a conclusion."

*Red flags:*
- "I publish it as established fact: 'Engineers cost 40% more'" (no uncertainty framing)
- "I ignore that it might be wrong" (no validation)
- "I hide the AI methodology" (black box)

---

**Q3: An AI tool generates a take on 'Why Recruitment Infrastructure Outperforms Agencies.' It's 80% good but makes a claim that conflicts with CMO positioning. What do you do?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "I flag the conflict with CMO immediately. Even if the insight is valuable, positioning misalignment is a blocker. CMO decides: update positioning or reframe the insight?"
- "I edit out the conflicting claim and submit the 80% good insight to CMO for approval before publishing."

*Red flags:*
- "I publish it as-is because it's good content" (no CMO approval gate)
- "I rewrite the claim to match positioning without telling CMO" (subtle positioning drift)
- "I assume the AI is right and CMO positioning is outdated" (role inversion)

## CRO Sub-Agents Discovery Q&A

### SDR Discovery Questions

**Q1: You've researched a prospect (Founder, 40-person SaaS company). Your data shows: Growing rapidly (4 hires/month = 50/year), currently using 3 agencies, annual spend likely £50K+. Your first email subject line is:**

*Option A:* "Stop overpaying recruitment agencies"
*Option B:* "Your hiring cost structure"
*Option C:* "Quick question about your recruitment ops"

*Good answers look like:*
- "Option B. It's data-driven, not competitive. It shows I did homework (I understand their cost structure), and it's consultative not pushy."
- "Option A is the old playbook. We position as consultative expert, not agency competitor."
- "Option C is too vague; it doesn't show why I'm reaching out."

*Red flags:*
- "Option A because it's bold" (vendor pitch language, contradicts CRO constraints)
- "Option C because it's soft-touch" (missing the consultative opportunity)
- "Any subject is fine as long as I follow up" (missing precision)

---

**Q2: Your first email (following PP-Email p1 framework) mentions that research shows agencies typically cost 20% of salary per hire. The prospect replies: "Where's this data from?" What do you do?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "I reply with sources: 'Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2024; Payscale industry reports, 2025; SHRM data.' I'm transparent. This builds trust and positions us as data-driven."
- "I send a one-pager: 'Recruitment Cost Benchmarks' with citations. The goal is to be the expert in the room, not the vendor."

*Red flags:*
- "I make up sources or cite proprietary Exprs data" (loses credibility)
- "I say 'I read it somewhere' or 'that's industry standard'" (vague)
- "I ignore the question and push to the next step" (loses the consultative moment)

---

**Q3: You've sent 3 emails in the PP-Email chain (p1, p2, p3). No reply. Your p4 (final email) is due. What do you do?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "I check the template: P4 should be a low-pressure exit. 'It seems now isn't the right time, but if your recruitment challenges shift, we're here.' Not: 'Your loss!' or 'Last chance!'"
- "Before sending p4, I check with CRO: Is there new insight (e.g., they just hired, so they're mid-growth phase)? If so, CRO might say 'refresh p1 with new angle' instead of p4 exit."

*Red flags:*
- "I send p4 aggressively: 'You're leaving money on the table'" (violates consultative tone)
- "I skip p4 and go straight to a sales call request" (wrong sequence)
- "I assume the prospect isn't interested" (premature exit)

### Sales Researcher Discovery Questions

**Q1: You're researching a prospect (CFO, 60-person fintech company). What's the #1 thing you need to find to personalize the outreach?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "Hiring velocity and likely agency spend. If they're growing rapidly, they're in the 'infrastructure ROI zone' — that's my angle. If they're stable, I need a different pain signal."
- "Evidence they're frustrated with current recruitment process. Maybe a recent job posting that's been open 60+ days, or multiple recruiters on LinkedIn, signaling frustration."
- "Their CFO's background: Do they have finance infrastructure experience (cloud, data tools)? If yes, I frame Exprs as infrastructure like they already understand."

*Red flags:*
- "The company's recent funding round" (funding ≠ recruitment pain signal)
- "Number of employees" (too broad; growth rate is what matters)
- "What they could theoretically save" (I don't calculate savings; I ask questions)

---

**Q2: You're building a deal strategy memo for a prospect who's been at 25 employees for 18 months (no growth). CRO is excited about the deal. Your memo says:**

*Option A:* "Low hiring velocity = low priority. Skip this deal."
*Option B:* "No growth signals; likely stable headcount planning. Pitch: long-term recruitment stability, not growth scaling."
*Option C:* "Data is unclear. Need to talk to the CFO first to understand their hiring strategy."

*Good answers look like:*
- "Option B. Just because they're not growing NOW doesn't mean they won't. But our pitch needs to shift. We're selling 'predictable costs even at stable volume' not 'infrastructure for scaling.'"
- "Option C, then followed by Option B. We can't assume; we need to discover their strategy first."
- "Option A is too dismissive. Stability is a legitimate use case for infrastructure."

*Red flags:*
- "Option A because they're not scaling" (missing opportunity; stability is still a pain point)
- "Option B without discovery first" (assuming strategy without asking)
- "I research until I'm 100% certain of their strategy" (analysis paralysis)

---

**Q3: You've researched a prospect and found conflicting signals: They're growing rapidly (good signal), but they recently hired an internal recruiter (seems like they're building in-house). What do you tell CRO?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "Interesting tension worth exploring in discovery. Maybe internal recruiter is stretched (opportunity for infrastructure). Maybe they're doubling down on in-house (we're a supplement, not replacement). Our angle: internal recruiter + infrastructure = high-impact pairing."
- "I note for CRO: 'Recent internal hire suggests they're betting on in-house. Our pitch: complement their recruiter with infrastructure, not replace the person.'"

*Red flags:*
- "They have a recruiter, so they're not a fit" (missing the play; internal recruiter + infrastructure is actually a strong pairing)
- "Ignore this signal and pitch as if they don't have one" (selective research)
- "Assume the internal recruiter will eventually fail and they'll buy infrastructure" (wrong positioning)

### Account Executive Discovery Questions

**Q1: You're on a discovery call. Founder says: "Our agencies aren't perfect, but we're getting placements. Why should I change?" How do you respond?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "I don't pitch change. I ask: 'Of those placements, how many stay longer than 18 months? What's your repeat-hire rate for the same role? Do you find yourself re-screening the same types of candidates across placements?' — I'm diagnosing the Steady-State problem without naming it."
- "I use data: 'Research shows companies lose 40% of recruiter knowledge with each placement. Have you noticed that?'"
- "I ask permission to explore: 'Would it be useful to map where agencies work best (surgical) vs. where infrastructure would compound efficiency (systemic)?'"

*Red flags:*
- "Your agencies are inefficient; we're better" (competitive pitch, not consultative)
- "Switch to us and save 30%" (claims without discovery context)
- "Agencies don't own your playbooks" (true, but leads with product benefit, not pain diagnosis)

---

**Q2: You're in a pricing conversation. Founder says: "£36K annually is more than we spend on ONE agency. So it seems expensive." Your response is:**

*Option A:* "But you use 3 agencies, so you're actually spending £50K+"
*Option B:* "You're comparing wrong. You should compare fixed annual cost to total annual spend, not to one agency fee."
*Option C:* "The value is in compound efficiency over time. By month 12, you'll have saved enough in time to justify the cost."

*Good answers look like:*
- "Option A. Use data they already have. '3 agencies x 20% fees on typical placements = £50K+. Fixed infrastructure is £36K, fixed. Let's model the comparison.'"
- "Option B, then follow with detailed TCO comparison. Frame it: 'Fixed cost vs. variable scaling.'"
- "Option A is the strongest because it uses their own data, not our projection."

*Red flags:*
- "Option C (future value is speculative; use current data)"
- "It's cheaper than you think" (vague)
- "We'll give you a discount to start" (compromises positioning)

---

**Q3: Prospect wants to "try it for one month." Your response is:**

*Good answers look like:*
- "I use the framework from CMO: 'Infrastructure needs 90 days to compound. Month one is setup and calibration. Month two is optimization. Month three is where compound efficiency becomes visible. We recommend a 90-day commitment with clear milestones.' Then I map what success looks like at each stage."
- "I ask: 'What would success look like in month one that would convince you to commit to 90 days?' Then I design the onboarding to hit those milestones."

*Red flags:*
- "Yes, we can do one month" (compromises infrastructure framing; infrastructure doesn't work at one-month scale)
- "One month at a higher price" (discounting model, not addressing root issue)
- "I'll push hard on 90 days but settle for one month" (no floor; positioning gets diluted)

---

**Q4: You've closed a deal. What's in your handoff documentation to Customer Success?**

*Good answers look like:*
- "Buyer pain points we uncovered (e.g., 'Lost 30% of recruiter knowledge each time agency changed'), pricing justification (why we chose £36K vs. negotiating down), objections we addressed (internal recruiter exists, is infrastructure a supplement?), and 90-day milestones (templates built, first hire screened, process optimized)."
- "I include: Their ICP (who are they hiring?), their current agency setup (so CS understands baseline), and the deal story (why they chose us, what they're trying to solve)."

*Red flags:*
- "Just the contract and price" (minimal handoff)
- "A summary of what AE promised" (should be about what CUSTOMER needs)
- "No structure; rambling notes" (CS needs clarity)

## Validation Scoring

**For each agent, score responses:**

| Score | Meaning | Action |
|-------|---------|--------|
| **3 Good** | Agent is fit; ready for Phase 2 spawning | ✅ APPROVE |
| **2 Good, 1 Red Flag** | Agent understands role but may need constraint clarification | ⚠️ CLARIFY with RPBW, then spawn |
| **1 Good, 2+ Red Flag** | Agent role/constraints may be misaligned with actual needs | ❌ REDESIGN SPAWN.md, then reassess |
| **0 Good** | Agent is not fit; recommend role rethinking or re-staffing | ❌ REJECT; escalate to RPBW |

---

## Remediation Path

**If agent scores ⚠️ or ❌:**

1. **Identify the misalignment:** Which constraint/deliverable is misunderstood?
2. **Redesign SPAWN.md:** Update constraint or role definition to clarify
3. **Re-assess:** Ask 2-3 new questions focused on the gap
4. **Decision point:** RPBW decides: spawn with updated constraints, or defer this role
